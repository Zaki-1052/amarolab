# Griffiths et al. (2016) — Psilocybin produces substantial and sustained decreases in depression and anxiety in patients with life-threatening cancer

**Citation:** Griffiths RR, Johnson MW, Carducci MA, Umbricht A, Richards WA, Richards BD, Cosimano MP, Klinedinst MA. *Journal of Psychopharmacology* 30(12): 1181–1197.

---

## One-Sentence Takeaway

A single high dose of psilocybin — administered under carefully controlled, psychologically supportive conditions — produced large, clinically significant, and sustained reductions in depression and anxiety in cancer patients, with roughly 80% of participants showing meaningful improvement at 6-month follow-up.

---

## Background & Motivation

When someone receives a life-threatening cancer diagnosis, the psychological impact can be as debilitating as the disease itself. Up to 40% of cancer patients meet criteria for a mood disorder, and chronic depression and anxiety are associated with worse treatment adherence, longer hospital stays, reduced quality of life, and even increased mortality. Standard treatments — antidepressants and benzodiazepines — show only limited, conflicting evidence of efficacy in this population, and benzodiazepines carry significant risks of dependence and side effects.

Psilocybin is the active compound in "magic mushrooms." Inside the body, it is rapidly dephosphorylated to psilocin, which acts as a potent agonist at the 5-HT2A serotonin receptor — the same receptor that is central to the BioChemCore program's focus. Psilocin's binding to 5-HT2A receptors in cortical and subcortical brain regions produces profound alterations in perception, cognition, and emotion. This mechanism is why classic psychedelics like psilocybin and LSD are structurally diverse yet functionally related: they all act as 5-HT2A agonists.

Research on psychedelics in therapeutic contexts was essentially halted for about three decades after the 1970s. By the 2000s, safety had been re-established in healthy volunteer studies, and two small earlier trials in cancer patients had shown promising trends toward decreased distress. However, those earlier studies lacked the rigorous comparison conditions required by modern clinical trial standards. The present study from the Johns Hopkins group is the most rigorous evaluation to date at the time of publication: a randomized, double-blind, crossover trial in 51 patients, carefully designed to minimize the powerful expectancy effects that confound psychedelic research.

---

## Approach & Methods

### Study design

The trial used a **double-blind, crossover design** — meaning every participant received both a very low dose and a high dose of psilocybin, just at different times, five weeks apart. This is a clever workaround for the fundamental problem in psychedelic research: psilocybin produces effects so distinctive and unmistakable that a standard placebo (like a sugar pill) would fool almost no one. By comparing a very low dose (1 or 3 mg/70 kg body weight, intended to be pharmacologically nearly inactive) against a therapeutically meaningful high dose (22 or 30 mg/70 kg), the researchers created the most defensible comparison condition available while still maintaining blinding as best as possible.

Participants were randomly assigned to receive either the low dose first or the high dose first. This allowed the researchers to compare the groups directly after Session 1 (when one group had received high dose and the other low dose), and then to track how the low-dose group changed after crossing over to the high dose.

### Participants

51 adults with a life-threatening cancer diagnosis who also met DSM-IV criteria for an anxiety or mood disorder. The group was mostly White (94%), highly educated (53% post-graduate), and had a mean age of about 56 years. Diagnoses included major depressive disorder, generalized anxiety disorder, chronic adjustment disorder, and dysthymia — all conditions where the cancer diagnosis had precipitated or worsened psychiatric symptoms.

### Minimizing expectancy effects

A major methodological innovation was the effort to minimize expectancy effects — the well-documented phenomenon where participants' and staff's prior beliefs about what a drug will do can substantially influence outcomes, especially with psychedelics. Participants and monitors were told that psilocybin would be administered in both sessions, that doses might range from very low to very high, that dose sensitivity varies across people, and that at least one session would be moderate to high. This created genuine uncertainty about which session contained the therapeutic dose. Neither participants nor monitors were told the actual doses being administered.

### The sessions themselves

Each psilocybin session was conducted in an aesthetically designed, comfortable living-room-like environment. Two trained session monitors were present throughout the 6–8 hour session. Participants were encouraged to lie down, wear an eye mask to block external distractions, and listen to a carefully curated music program through headphones. They were encouraged to focus inward on their own experience rather than on their illness specifically. The monitors were supportive and non-directive — their role was to create psychological safety, not to direct the experience's content.

This "set and setting" framework — controlling the psychological context in which the drug is taken — is considered essential to achieving therapeutic outcomes rather than distressing ones. It reflects the field's understanding that psilocybin's effects are not purely pharmacological; they are deeply shaped by the individual's mindset and environment.

### Outcome measures

The study was unusually thorough in its measurement. The two primary clinical outcomes were:
- **GRID-HAM-D-17**: a standard clinician-rated depression scale
- **HAM-A**: a clinician-rated anxiety scale

These were supplemented by 15 secondary measures covering self-rated depression and anxiety, mood disturbance, quality of life, life meaning, optimism, death acceptance, and spirituality. Assessments occurred at baseline, 5 weeks after each session, and 6 months after Session 2. Community observers (family members, friends, coworkers) who were blind to the study also rated participants' attitudes and behaviors, providing an independent external check.

A particularly important additional measure was the **Mystical Experience Questionnaire (MEQ30)**, which quantifies the intensity of psilocybin-occasioned mystical-type experiences — feelings of unity, transcendence of time and space, deeply felt sacredness, and noetic quality. The authors had previously shown that the intensity of mystical experience predicts long-term positive changes in attitude and well-being in healthy volunteers, and they wanted to test whether this relationship held in a therapeutic context.

## Key Findings

### Large and rapid therapeutic effects

High-dose psilocybin produced dramatic and statistically robust reductions in depression and anxiety. At 5 weeks after the high-dose session:

- **92% of participants in the High-Dose-1st group showed a clinically significant response** on the GRID-HAM-D-17 depression scale (defined as ≥50% decrease from baseline), compared to only 32% after the low dose.
- For anxiety (HAM-A), **76% showed a clinically significant response** after the high dose vs. 24% after the low dose.
- **Symptom remission** (returning to the normal range on both the score threshold and the absolute scale) was achieved by 60% on depression and 52% on anxiety after the high dose.

These are very large effects. Cohen's d effect sizes for the between-group difference at Post-session 1 averaged 0.82 across the 11 primary measures — this is considered a "large" effect by conventional benchmarks, and it compares favorably to antidepressants, which typically achieve effect sizes around 0.3–0.4 in similar populations.

### Effects were sustained at 6 months

This is arguably the most striking finding. The improvements were not a transient afterglow of an unusual experience; they endured. At 6-month follow-up:

- 78% of participants showed a clinically significant response on the GRID-HAM-D-17 depression scale.
- 83% showed a clinically significant response on the HAM-A anxiety scale.
- Overall symptom remission rates were 65% for depression and 57% for anxiety.
- The mean effect size for the difference between Baseline and 6 months (collapsed across groups) was 1.55 — an extraordinarily large sustained effect.

Improvements were also seen on nearly all secondary measures, including quality of life, life meaning, optimism, and reduced fear of death (death acceptance).

### Community observers independently confirmed the changes

Family members, friends, and colleagues — who had no knowledge of the study design — also rated significant improvements in participants' attitudes and behaviors at both post-session timepoints. This provides an important external validity check: the improvements were not just self-reported or clinician-rated based on participant responses in the research context; they were visible in everyday life.

### The mystical experience was a key mediator

The intensity of the psilocybin-occasioned mystical experience during Session 1 (measured by MEQ30) was significantly correlated with therapeutic benefit at 5 weeks across 18 of 20 outcome measures. The stronger the mystical experience a participant had during the session, the greater their subsequent reduction in anxiety (r = −0.59) and depression (r = −0.36), and the greater their increases in life meaning (r = 0.77) and spiritual significance (r = 0.75).

Critically, a **mediation analysis** showed that mystical experience score statistically mediated the effect of psilocybin dose on seven key therapeutic outcomes — including anxiety, depression, and life meaning. This is not just a correlation; the data suggest that inducing a profound mystical-type experience is part of the causal pathway through which psilocybin produces therapeutic benefit. This is a deeply unusual finding in pharmacology and has significant implications for how we think about the mechanism of action.

Even when controlling for overall drug effect intensity (to separate "how strong was the experience" from "how mystical was the experience"), mystical experience continued to predict outcomes on 11 of the 18 measures. This suggests mystical experience contributes something specific beyond just the magnitude of the pharmacological effect.

### Persisting changes in attitudes and spirituality

At 6 months, participants attributed significant positive changes to the high-dose experience across multiple domains: positive attitudes about life and self, positive mood changes, altruistic/social effects, positive behavior changes, and increased spirituality. Over 80% endorsed moderately or greater increased well-being or life satisfaction. Negative ratings of these same dimensions were low and did not differ meaningfully between dose conditions.

### Safety profile

No serious adverse events attributed to psilocybin occurred. The adverse effects that did occur were transient and resolved by end of session:
- Elevated systolic blood pressure (>160 mmHg) occurred in 34% of high-dose sessions (17% low dose)
- Nausea/vomiting in 15% of high-dose sessions
- Psychological discomfort in 32% of high-dose sessions
- Anxiety in 26% of high-dose sessions
- One episode of transient paranoid ideation (2% of high-dose sessions)

There were no cases of hallucinogen-persisting perception disorder or prolonged psychosis. The profile is consistent with what had been established in earlier safety studies in healthy volunteers.

## Significance & Implications

### For the treatment of cancer-related distress

This paper is one of the most influential modern clinical trials of a psychedelic compound and helped catalyze the current wave of psilocybin research. The effect sizes are large relative to existing pharmacological and psychotherapeutic options for cancer-related depression and anxiety. The fact that a single administered dose produces effects lasting at least 6 months — with no ongoing medication — is essentially without precedent in psychopharmacology.

### For understanding the 5-HT2A receptor

This paper is directly relevant to the BioChemCore program's focus on the 5-HT2A receptor. Psilocin (psilocybin's active metabolite) is a prototypical 5-HT2A agonist, and its therapeutic effects here are attributed to 5-HT2A receptor activation in cortical and subcortical circuits. The paper frames psilocybin as acting "directly and indirectly on various brain cortical and subcortical areas" and altering "brain network dynamics" through 5-HT2A agonism. The question of exactly which downstream signaling cascades — Gq, Gi, beta-arrestin, and so on — underlie the therapeutic vs. hallucinogenic effects is precisely what more recent papers in the BioChemCore reading list (such as Xu 2026, Jastrzębski 2025, Kossatz 2024) are beginning to disentangle at the molecular level.

### For the question of mechanism: pharmacology vs. experience

The mystical experience mediation finding raises a profound question that sits at the intersection of pharmacology and psychology: is the therapeutic mechanism primarily a downstream pharmacological effect on receptor-mediated neuroplasticity, or does it require the subjective experience itself? The authors note they cannot resolve this with behavioral data alone, and that "precisely how the enduring therapeutically relevant psilocybin effects are reflected in long-term alteration of cortical networks or other neuroplastic changes remains to be established." This is the gap that papers like Ly 2018 (structural neuroplasticity) and Olson 2022 (biochemical mechanisms) in the reading list begin to address.

### Connection to BioChemCore

When you are simulating the 5-HT2A receptor in a membrane and analyzing its conformational dynamics, this paper provides the clinical "why it matters" context. The receptor you are modeling is the molecular switch through which psilocybin exerts these therapeutic effects. Understanding how ligand binding changes receptor conformation, which G-protein couples, and which signaling pathways are activated vs. biased is directly relevant to understanding why psilocybin works, whether it can be made safer (non-hallucinogenic but therapeutically active), and how drug design might optimize for therapeutic outcomes. The connection runs from the molecular dynamics trajectory on your screen all the way to the patients in this trial.

## Limitations & Open Questions

### The low-dose comparator is not a true placebo

The most important methodological limitation: 1–3 mg/70 kg psilocybin is a real drug at a real (if low) dose. It produces detectable subjective effects. This means the "placebo" condition was not pharmacologically inert, which complicates interpretation of the between-group comparison. The authors acknowledge this and note that the low dose may have some therapeutic pharmacological activity of its own, which would bias results toward the null (meaning true effect sizes could be even larger than observed).

### Crossover design and post-5-week comparisons

Because participants crossed over to the alternative dose condition after 5 weeks, the study could only make rigorous double-blind between-group comparisons at the 5-week post-Session-1 timepoint. The 6-month data are based on within-subject comparisons from baseline, not blinded between-group comparisons. This is a real limitation — the sustained 6-month effects are compelling but cannot be attributed solely to the high dose via a blinded comparison.

### Sample is not representative

The sample was predominantly White (94%), highly educated (53% post-graduate), middle-aged, and selected based on willingness to participate in psychedelic research. Generalizability to more diverse, less educated, or less psychedelically-curious cancer populations is unknown.

### Expectancy effects cannot be fully eliminated

Despite creative blinding procedures, most monitors (5 of 8) correctly identified that psilocybin had been administered. Participants' expectations about what psilocybin might do — positive or negative — almost certainly shaped their experience to some degree. This is an inherent challenge in psychedelic research that cannot be fully solved by blinding.

### Mechanism remains unclear

The paper demonstrates efficacy but cannot establish mechanism. Whether the therapeutic benefit derives primarily from 5-HT2A-mediated neuroplasticity, from the subjective mystical experience altering psychological frameworks, from some interaction between the two, or from other receptor targets entirely (5-HT1A, 5-HT2C, etc.) remains an open question.

### Small n and single site

With 51 participants at a single institution (Johns Hopkins), the study is underpowered for subgroup analyses and lacks the external validity of a multisite trial. The authors explicitly call for a larger, more diverse multisite study.

## Key Terms & Concepts

**Psilocybin / Psilocin:** Psilocybin is the prodrug compound found in "magic mushrooms"; it is rapidly converted in the body to psilocin, its active metabolite, which crosses the blood-brain barrier and acts as a 5-HT2A receptor agonist.

**5-HT2A receptor:** A G-protein coupled receptor (GPCR) that responds to serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine) and is the primary molecular target through which classic psychedelics produce their psychological effects. It is highly expressed in cortical pyramidal neurons and is central to the BioChemCore program.

**Mystical-type experience (MEQ30):** A specific category of profoundly altered subjective state characterized by feelings of unity, transcendence of time and space, sacredness, deeply felt positive mood, and a sense of encountering ultimate reality. Measured in this study by the 30-item Mystical Experience Questionnaire. Intensity of this experience was the strongest predictor of therapeutic outcome.

**Crossover design:** A clinical trial design where each participant receives multiple treatments (in different sequences) rather than being assigned to only one. Allows each participant to serve as their own control, increasing statistical power but introducing order effects and limiting blinded comparisons after the first crossover.

**Clinical response vs. symptom remission:** Clinical response (used here: ≥50% decrease from baseline on a rating scale) means meaningful improvement. Symptom remission (≥50% decrease plus a score at or below the normal range threshold) means returning to essentially healthy functioning. Remission is the higher bar.

**GRID-HAM-D-17:** The Hamilton Depression Rating Scale — a standard clinician-administered interview that quantifies the severity of depressive symptoms across 17 items. One of the two primary outcome measures in this study.

**Mediation analysis:** A statistical approach that tests whether a third variable (here, mystical experience intensity) explains the causal pathway between an independent variable (psilocybin dose) and an outcome (therapeutic benefit). Finding that MEQ30 score mediated the effect of dose suggests mystical experience is not just correlated with benefit but is part of how psilocybin produces it.
