# Ly et al. 2018 — Psychedelics Promote Structural and Functional Neural Plasticity

**Citation:** Ly C, Greb AC, Cameron LP, Wong JM, Barragan EV, Wilson PC, Burbach KF, Soltanzadeh Zarandi S, Sood A, Paddy MR, Duim WC, Dennis MY, McAllister AK, Ori-McKenney KM, Gray JA, Olson DE. *Cell Reports.* 2018 Jun 12; 23(11):3170–3182.

---

## One-Sentence Takeaway

Serotonergic psychedelics — including LSD, DMT, and DOI — promote growth of dendritic arbors, dendritic spines, and synapses in cortical neurons through TrkB, mTOR, and 5-HT2A receptor signaling, rivaling or exceeding the effects of the fast-acting antidepressant ketamine.

---

## Background & Motivation

Depression and related neuropsychiatric disorders are among the leading causes of disability worldwide. A foundational — and often underappreciated — insight in modern psychiatry is that depression is not simply a chemical imbalance of neurotransmitters. Rather, significant evidence from human brain imaging, postmortem studies, and animal models points to **physical atrophy of neurons in the prefrontal cortex (PFC)** as a key contributor to the disease. Neurons in the PFC lose their dendritic branches (neurites), their dendritic spines (tiny protrusions where synaptic connections form), and ultimately their synapses altogether. This structural shrinkage is worsened by chronic stress and correlates with severity of symptoms.

The therapeutic implication is powerful: if you could reverse this atrophy — grow back the branches and spines — you might restore healthy brain function. This idea is called **neuroplasticity-based treatment**, and it has become one of the most exciting frontiers in psychiatry.

The gold standard example of a plasticity-promoting drug is **ketamine**, a dissociative anesthetic that acts on NMDA glutamate receptors. Ketamine produces rapid antidepressant effects within hours rather than weeks — a dramatic departure from conventional antidepressants — and it promotes dendritic spine growth in the PFC. However, ketamine has significant drawbacks: it is addictive, it has dissociative side effects, and its mechanism is not fully understood.

At the same time, classical serotonergic psychedelics (LSD, psilocybin, DMT) and entactogens (MDMA) have been showing remarkable results in clinical trials for depression and PTSD, with effects that persist long after the acute drug experience has ended. Yet nobody had rigorously tested whether these compounds also promote structural neural plasticity in the way ketamine does. That is the gap this paper addresses.

The authors hypothesized that psychedelics and ketamine share a common therapeutic mechanism: both promote structural and functional neuroplasticity in the PFC. They coined the term **"psychoplastogen"** (from Greek: psych- for mind, -plast for molded, -gen for producing) to describe any compound capable of rapidly inducing structural and functional neural plasticity.

---

## Approach & Methods

The research team used a combination of cell culture models, in vivo rodent studies, and invertebrate (Drosophila) experiments to build a multi-level case for psychoplastogenesis.

**In vitro neuritogenesis assays.** The team cultured primary cortical neurons from embryonic day 18 rat embryos. At day 6 in vitro (DIV6 — young neurons still extending their initial processes), they applied drugs and then used **Sholl analysis** to quantify dendritic arbor complexity. Sholl analysis works by drawing concentric circles around the neuron's cell body and counting how many dendrite crossings fall at each radius — a more complex, branchy tree will have more crossings at greater distances. This allowed precise, quantitative comparison across compounds and concentrations.

**Spinogenesis and synaptogenesis.** On more mature neurons (DIV19), the team used **super-resolution structured illumination microscopy (SIM)** to image dendritic spines at nanoscale resolution. This matters because dendritic spines are tiny (roughly 0.5–2 micrometers), and conventional light microscopy cannot reliably count them. SIM allowed accurate quantification of spine density and classification of spine types (thin and filopodium = immature; mushroom = mature). For synaptogenesis, they used co-labeling with the presynaptic marker VGLUT1 and the postsynaptic marker PSD-95 — co-localization of these two markers indicates an actual synapse has formed.

**In vivo confirmation.** To verify findings in a living animal, they gave adult rats a single intraperitoneal (IP) injection of DMT (10 mg/kg) and examined PFC neurons 24 hours later using **Golgi-Cox staining** (a classical technique that stains a small random subset of neurons black, making them visible for morphological analysis). They also performed **ex vivo slice electrophysiology** to measure functional synaptic output.

**Mechanism dissection.** To identify which signaling pathways were responsible, the authors used selective pharmacological blockers pretreated before psychedelic application:
- **ANA-12** (a selective TrkB antagonist — TrkB is the receptor for the neurotrophin BDNF)
- **Rapamycin** (an mTOR inhibitor — mTOR is a master regulator of protein synthesis and cell growth)
- **Ketanserin** (a selective 5-HT2A antagonist at low concentrations)

**Cross-species validation.** They treated Drosophila larvae with LSD and DOI to test whether plasticity effects were conserved across vertebrate and invertebrate nervous systems — a key test for whether the mechanism is fundamental or species-specific.

## Key Findings

### 1. Psychedelics robustly promote neuritogenesis in cortical neurons

Nearly all tested psychedelics — spanning three distinct chemical classes (tryptamines: DMT and psilocin; amphetamines: DOI and MDMA; ergolines: LSD) — significantly increased dendritic arbor complexity in cultured cortical neurons compared to vehicle control. The effect was comparable to that produced by ketamine (10 µM). The structural changes were primarily driven by increased dendritic branching and total arbor length, not changes in the number of primary dendrites or the length of the longest dendrite.

Potency comparisons were particularly striking: ketamine's EC50 (half-maximal effective concentration) was 132 nM, but most psychedelics were significantly more potent. LSD was extraordinarily potent, with an EC50 of just 0.409 nM — activity spanning 8 orders of magnitude into the low picomolar range. The authors speculate this extreme potency may result from LSD's known very slow dissociation from serotonin receptors (its "slow off kinetics"), a property revealed by the crystal structure of LSD bound to the 5-HT2B receptor.

Two important negative controls established specificity: serotonin itself and D-amphetamine (a stimulant chemically related to the amphetamine-class psychedelics but non-hallucinogenic) produced minimal to no neuritogenesis. This rules out the explanation that any monoamine-related compound would work — the effect is specific to the psychoplastogen class.

**Ibogaine** (the anti-addictive alkaloid) was the only tested psychedelic with absolutely no effect. However, **noribogaine**, its primary active metabolite in vivo, robustly promoted dendritic arbor complexity with an EC50 comparable to ketamine — suggesting noribogaine is the active form responsible for ibogaine's neuroplastic effects.

### 2. Psychedelics promote spinogenesis and synaptogenesis

On mature cortical neurons (DIV19), all three tested representatives — DOI, DMT, and LSD — increased dendritic spine density, as measured by super-resolution SIM. LSD was particularly striking, nearly doubling the number of spines per 10 µm of dendrite. Drug treatment also shifted the distribution of spine types toward immature forms (thin and filopodium), suggesting ongoing dynamic remodeling rather than simple spine stabilization.

Crucially, psychedelics also increased the number of functional synapses: co-labeling with VGLUT1 (presynaptic) and PSD-95 (postsynaptic) showed increased synapse density after treatment. Importantly, synapse size was not changed — only the number increased. The increase in VGLUT1 puncta (but not PSD-95 puncta) following treatment suggests that new presynaptic terminals are forming at existing postsynaptic sites, or that VGLUT1+ terminals are being newly recruited.

### 3. DMT replicates ketamine's in vivo effects on the PFC

A single IP dose of DMT (10 mg/kg) significantly increased dendritic spine density on cortical pyramidal neurons in the rat PFC 24 hours after dosing, as measured by Golgi-Cox staining. The magnitude of this effect was comparable to ketamine at the same dose. This is notable because DMT has a half-life of only ~15 minutes in the body — the structural changes persist for hours well after the drug has been cleared.

These structural changes were accompanied by functional changes. Ex vivo slice recordings of layer V pyramidal neurons in the PFC showed that DMT treatment (both 10 mg/kg and 1 mg/kg — the latter being a subhallucinogenic dose) increased both the frequency and amplitude of spontaneous excitatory postsynaptic currents (sEPSCs). Increased frequency suggests more active presynaptic inputs; increased amplitude suggests stronger individual synaptic connections. Both doses produced similar magnitudes of effect, which is significant: even a dose too low to cause hallucinogenic behavior still promotes functional synaptic plasticity.

### 4. The plasticity requires TrkB, mTOR, and 5-HT2A signaling

The mechanistic dissection was clean and decisive:

- **TrkB blockade with ANA-12** completely prevented both psychedelic-induced and BDNF-induced neuritogenesis and spinogenesis. This places TrkB — the high-affinity BDNF receptor — as a required node in the pathway. Interestingly, while psychedelics did not increase BDNF mRNA (measured by ddPCR), they did produce an approximately 2-fold increase in BDNF protein levels (measured by ELISA, though this did not reach statistical significance). The combination of BDNF plus psychedelic did not add further above either alone, consistent with them operating through the same TrkB pathway.

- **mTOR inhibition with rapamycin** completely blocked psychedelic-induced neuritogenesis. This is consistent with the established pathway: TrkB activation leads to PI3K-Akt-mTOR signaling, which drives local protein synthesis in dendrites necessary for structural remodeling.

- **5-HT2A blockade with ketanserin** completely blocked the ability of DMT, LSD, DOI, psilocin, noribogaine, and MDMA to promote neuritogenesis and spinogenesis. At low LSD concentrations (10 nM), 10 nM ketanserin partially blocked (~50%) and 100 nM ketanserin completely prevented neuritogenesis, consistent with ketanserin acting selectively on 5-HT2A at these concentrations. This establishes that 5-HT2A receptor activation is required upstream of the TrkB/mTOR cascade.

The polypharmacology of psychedelics (Hill slopes deviating from 1.0 in dose-response curves) suggests other receptors modulate effects at higher concentrations, but 5-HT2A is the primary mediator at therapeutic concentrations.

## Significance & Implications

### For psychiatric drug development

This paper provides the first rigorous, multi-model demonstration that classical serotonergic psychedelics are psychoplastogens — a class that previously included only ketamine and a few other compounds. By establishing that diverse chemical scaffolds (tryptamines, ergolines, amphetamines) all produce this effect, the paper opens up an enormous medicinal chemistry space for developing next-generation antidepressants.

The finding that subhallucinogenic doses of DMT produce functional plasticity is especially important. It raises the possibility that the hallucinogenic experience itself is not required for the therapeutic structural benefit — a question with profound implications for whether non-hallucinogenic analogs could work as antidepressants. The authors explicitly flag this as an active area of their work.

### For the BioChemCore program

This paper is directly relevant to your study of the 5-HT2A serotonin receptor. The 5-HT2A receptor is the key upstream mediator of psychoplastogenesis in this paper — blocking it with ketanserin abolishes all psychedelic-induced structural plasticity. The receptor is a GPCR (G protein-coupled receptor), a Class A membrane protein that sits in the post-synaptic membrane of cortical pyramidal neurons (exactly the type of neuron described here).

When you simulate the 5-HT2A receptor in a lipid bilayer using MD in BioChemCore, what you are modeling is precisely the receptor that gates this entire plasticity cascade. The signaling path is: ligand (LSD/DMT/etc.) binds 5-HT2A → Gq/11 protein activation → downstream TrkB/mTOR signaling → local protein synthesis → dendritic spine growth. Understanding the receptor's conformational states (active vs. inactive), ligand binding pocket geometry, and dynamics in the membrane is directly relevant to understanding why different psychedelics have such wildly different potencies (LSD EC50: 0.409 nM vs. ketamine EC50: 132 nM) and possibly why some compounds produce hallucinogenic effects and others do not.

The paper also connects to the broader "Biology first" mandate of BioChemCore: the post-synaptic membrane you are simulating is not a static structure but a biologically dynamic one where receptor activation drives large-scale structural remodeling.

### Connecting to the psychoplastogen concept

The authors introduce the term "psychoplastogen" here, which has since been adopted broadly in the field. This is a neat example of how a single well-executed paper can reframe an entire research area — from "psychedelics as hallucinogens" to "psychedelics as structural neuroplasticity agents" — by providing mechanistic molecular evidence.

## Limitations & Open Questions

**Extended in vitro exposure vs. short in vivo exposure.** The in vitro cell culture experiments applied psychedelics for extended periods (24 hours in most cases), whereas in vivo brain exposure is typically minutes to hours due to rapid metabolism. The authors acknowledge this mismatch and flag it as an important unresolved issue for understanding the kinetics of in vivo plasticity induction.

**Mechanistic ambiguity around TrkB activation.** The paper shows TrkB is required (blocking it blocks everything) but does not fully establish *how* psychedelics activate TrkB. Do they increase BDNF release, transactivate TrkB directly, or trigger some intermediate? The BDNF protein data trend toward an increase but does not reach statistical significance, leaving this open.

**5-HT2A vs. other serotonin receptors.** At high concentrations, ketanserin is less selective and could partially block 5-HT2C or other receptors. The polypharmacology issue (Hill slopes ≠ 1.0) suggests multiple receptors are engaged at higher doses. The paper is careful about this but cannot fully exclude contributions from 5-HT2C or adrenergic receptors at the doses used.

**Ibogaine vs. noribogaine.** The finding that ibogaine has no effect but noribogaine does is interesting and consistent with in vivo metabolic conversion, but the paper does not deeply explain the structural basis of why the metabolite is active and the parent compound is not.

**Non-hallucinogenic doses and clinical relevance.** The demonstration that subhallucinogenic DMT doses promote functional plasticity is tantalizing but limited to electrophysiology. Whether subhallucinogenic doses produce comparable structural (spine density) changes was not tested.

**Causation vs. correlation of plasticity and therapeutic effect.** The paper argues that plasticity is the mechanism of the antidepressant effect, and circumstantial evidence (behavioral studies from the same group, comparison with ketamine) supports this. But the paper does not directly demonstrate that blocking plasticity eliminates the behavioral antidepressant effect — that causal link remains to be fully established.

## Key Terms & Concepts

**Psychoplastogen** — A compound capable of rapidly inducing structural and functional neural plasticity; coined in this paper to classify compounds like ketamine, LSD, DMT, and related molecules by their shared biological action rather than their psychological effects.

**Neuritogenesis** — The growth and elaboration of neurites (axons and dendrites) from a neuron. In this context, specifically refers to increased dendritic branching and arbor complexity in cortical neurons.

**Spinogenesis** — The formation and growth of dendritic spines, the small protrusions on dendrites where most excitatory synapses form. Loss of spines is a hallmark of depression; psychedelics reverse this.

**Sholl analysis** — A quantitative method for measuring dendritic arbor complexity by counting how many dendritic branches cross concentric circles drawn at increasing radii from the cell body. Higher area under the curve = more complex, branchy tree.

**TrkB (Tropomyosin receptor kinase B)** — The primary high-affinity receptor for BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor). Its activation triggers PI3K-Akt-mTOR signaling cascades that drive protein synthesis and structural plasticity. Required for psychoplastogen effects.

**mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin)** — A serine/threonine kinase that acts as a central hub for integrating growth signals and driving protein synthesis. Downstream of TrkB in the plasticity cascade; rapamycin blocks it and completely prevents psychedelic-induced neuritogenesis.

**5-HT2A receptor** — A G protein-coupled serotonin receptor (Gq/11-coupled) expressed on cortical pyramidal neurons; the primary molecular target of classical serotonergic psychedelics and the gatekeeper of psychoplastogenesis. Blocking it with ketanserin abolishes all tested psychedelic structural plasticity effects.
