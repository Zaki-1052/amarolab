# Summary: Ley et al. 2023 — Comparative Acute Effects of Mescaline, LSD, and Psilocybin

**Citation:** Ley L, Holze F, Arikci D, et al. "Comparative acute effects of mescaline, lysergic acid diethylamide, and psilocybin in a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled cross-over study in healthy participants." *Neuropsychopharmacology* (2023) 48:1659–1667.

---

## 1. One-Sentence Takeaway

At psychoactive-equivalent doses, mescaline, LSD, and psilocybin produce indistinguishable subjective psychedelic experiences, differing meaningfully only in duration of action — a difference explained by pharmacokinetics rather than receptor pharmacology.

---

## 2. Background & Motivation

Mescaline, LSD, and psilocybin are the three most historically prominent "classic psychedelics," and all three are currently experiencing renewed scientific and therapeutic interest. They are used in religious ceremonies, recreationally, and increasingly in clinical trials for depression, anxiety, addiction, and other psychiatric conditions. Despite their long histories and structural diversity, a rigorous, direct, head-to-head comparison of all three substances in a controlled human study had never been done.

All three substances are classified as **serotonergic psychedelics** because their primary mechanism of action is agonism at the **5-HT2A receptor** — a subtype of serotonin receptor concentrated in cortical regions of the brain, particularly the prefrontal cortex. This is the molecular target most closely linked to psychedelic effects, visual hallucinations, and altered states of consciousness. This is the same receptor your BioChemCore program focuses on: the 5-HT2A receptor is a G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) embedded in the post-synaptic membrane, making it a direct subject of the kind of molecular dynamics simulations you are running.

However, beyond their shared 5-HT2A agonism, these three drugs are pharmacologically quite different from each other:

- **Mescaline** (from peyote and San Pedro cacti) also acts at 5-HT1A and adrenergic alpha-2A receptors, and is roughly 30–3000 times less potent than the other two — meaning it is used at dramatically higher doses (hundreds of milligrams vs. micrograms for LSD).
- **LSD** is the most potent and also binds dopamine D1-3 receptors and multiple 5-HT receptor subtypes.
- **Psilocybin** is a prodrug — it is metabolized in the body to its active form **psilocin**, which hits 5-HT2A and also inhibits the serotonin transporter (SERT), the protein responsible for clearing serotonin from the synapse.

Given these distinct receptor profiles, the researchers wanted to ask: do these pharmacological differences actually translate into meaningfully different subjective experiences? Or does shared 5-HT2A agonism dominate everything else? Beyond that practical question, they also wanted to establish dose equivalence — a necessary foundation for comparing results across separate clinical trials that may have used different substances.

---

## 3. Approach & Methods

The study used a **randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover design** — the gold standard for within-person comparisons of drug effects. Thirty-two healthy adults (16 men, 16 women, mean age 29) each attended four separate dosing sessions separated by at least 10 days, receiving one condition per session in randomized order: mescaline, LSD, psilocybin, or placebo. Each participant therefore served as their own control, which powerfully reduces confounds from individual differences in personality, baseline anxiety, and prior drug experience.

One design complication is worth noting: the researchers discovered partway through that 300 mg mescaline was too weak relative to LSD and psilocybin, so the first 16 participants received 300 mg mescaline and the second 16 received 500 mg. This means the mescaline dose comparison is between-subjects and was not randomized or blinded — a methodological limitation the authors acknowledge.

The final "equivalent" doses settled on were: **500 mg mescaline hydrochloride = 100 µg LSD = 20 mg psilocybin**.

Participants were monitored continuously for 24 hours per session in a calm hospital room. The study measured:

- **Subjective effects** using Visual Analog Scales (VAS) sampled repeatedly over 24 hours, plus validated psychometric tools — the 5 Dimensions of Altered States of Consciousness (5D-ASC), the Mystical Experience Questionnaire (MEQ), and the Adjective Mood Rating Scale (AMRS) — filled out retrospectively at 24 hours.
- **Autonomic effects**: blood pressure, heart rate, body temperature, and pupil diameter.
- **Pharmacokinetics**: plasma concentrations of mescaline, LSD, and psilocin (the active metabolite of psilocybin) plus their metabolites, measured via LC-MS/MS (liquid chromatography tandem mass spectrometry — a highly sensitive analytical technique for measuring drug levels in blood).
- **Biomarkers**: plasma oxytocin and BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), both of which have been proposed as mediators of psychedelic therapeutic effects.

The logic of including oxytocin and BDNF is worth explaining. **Oxytocin** is a neuropeptide involved in social bonding, trust, and empathy — mechanisms potentially relevant to psychedelic-assisted therapy. **BDNF** promotes neuroplasticity — the growth and rewiring of synaptic connections — and has been proposed as a mechanism by which psychedelics might produce lasting antidepressant effects.

## 4. Key Findings

### Subjective Effects: Largely Indistinguishable at Equivalent Doses

The headline result: at psychoactive-equivalent doses (500 mg mescaline, 100 µg LSD, 20 mg psilocybin), there were no statistically significant differences in the maximum subjective effect ratings across all four psychometric instruments. The three drugs produced equivalent peak scores on the VAS, the 5D-ASC (which measures oceanic boundlessness, anxious ego dissolution, and visionary restructuralization), and the MEQ (which measures mystical experience dimensions like transcendence and ineffability).

Notably, participants could not reliably distinguish which substance they had taken during the experience — correct identification rates ranged from 48–58% during the session, not far above chance for a four-choice question. Even after the study ended and participants had time to reflect, identification remained imperfect. The substances were most commonly mistaken for each other, not for placebo.

The 300 mg mescaline dose was clearly sub-equivalent — it produced weaker effects on all scales — which is why the dose was increased.

### Duration: A Clear and Meaningful Difference

The one major distinction between the three drugs was how long their effects lasted, and this difference was substantial:

- Mescaline (500 mg): mean duration **11.1 hours** (range 6–22 h)
- LSD (100 µg): mean duration **8.2 hours** (range 4.3–19 h)
- Psilocybin (20 mg): mean duration **4.9 hours** (range 2.6–10 h)

All three differences were statistically significant. Mescaline's longer duration was not because it was slower to leave the body — its plasma elimination half-life (3.6 h) was nearly identical to LSD's (3.5 h). Instead, mescaline took longer to reach peak plasma levels (Tmax ~2.3 h vs ~1.4 h for LSD) and had a broader plateau — it climbed more slowly, stayed near its peak longer, and only then declined at a similar rate to LSD. Psilocin had the shortest half-life at 2.3 h, consistent with psilocybin's noticeably shorter experience.

This is a pharmacokinetically elegant finding: the difference in total duration between mescaline and LSD is almost entirely explained by the time-to-peak difference, not by differential elimination.

### Autonomic Effects: Moderate and Broadly Similar

All three substances moderately increased systolic and diastolic blood pressure, body temperature, and pupil size compared to placebo. The only statistically significant difference between substances was that psilocybin produced a higher diastolic blood pressure increase than LSD. LSD showed a trend toward greater heart rate elevation than psilocybin. Overall cardiovascular stimulation (measured as the "rate pressure product," combining heart rate and blood pressure) was comparable across all three.

Mescaline was expected to show stronger cardiovascular effects because of its adrenergic activity, but this was not borne out — its autonomic profile was similar to the others.

### Adverse Effects

All three substances produced more adverse effects during the acute phase (0–12 hours) than placebo. Mescaline was uniquely notable for causing significant subacute adverse effects (12–24 hours after dosing) — likely because its longer duration of action simply means the "hangover" window extends further. Mescaline also caused more headaches, and two participants experienced persistent depressive symptoms lasting days to weeks (one after psilocybin, one after all three substances) — these resolved spontaneously and are consistent with what is already reported in the LSD safety literature. Four flashback phenomena occurred across the study. No serious adverse events were reported.

### Oxytocin and BDNF

Mescaline and LSD both significantly elevated plasma oxytocin levels compared to placebo. Mescaline was actually the strongest oxytocin releaser of the three. Psilocybin did not significantly increase oxytocin — a surprising finding given that some prior studies suggested it does. This is the first published report of mescaline-induced oxytocin release in humans.

None of the substances significantly altered plasma BDNF levels. The authors note that using plasma (rather than serum) samples may be a technical limitation that reduces BDNF detection sensitivity, so this null result should be interpreted cautiously.

## 5. Significance & Implications

### For Understanding 5-HT2A Pharmacology

This paper provides strong evidence that 5-HT2A agonism is the primary driver of the psychedelic experience across structurally diverse compounds. The fact that mescaline (a phenethylamine), LSD (an ergoline), and psilocin (a tryptamine) — three molecules from completely different chemical classes that happen to all activate 5-HT2A — produce subjectively indistinguishable altered states is a powerful argument that receptor engagement dominates over the other pharmacological differences between these compounds.

For your BioChemCore work specifically: this paper is a strong motivation for why studying the 5-HT2A receptor structure and dynamics is so important. The receptor itself, embedded in the post-synaptic membrane, is the convergence point where chemically diverse psychedelics produce a shared functional outcome. The structural details of how these drugs dock into the receptor's binding pocket — and how the receptor then couples to intracellular signaling proteins — matter enormously for understanding why agonism at this one receptor produces such dramatic effects on consciousness.

### Dose Equivalence for Clinical Research

The established equivalence (500 mg mescaline = 100 µg LSD = 20 mg psilocybin) is a practically important contribution. Clinical trials of psychedelic-assisted therapy often use different compounds, and without dose equivalence benchmarks it's difficult to compare outcomes across studies. This paper gives researchers a validated reference point.

### Differential Oxytocin Release

The finding that mescaline and LSD, but not psilocybin, elevate oxytocin is interesting because oxytocin is thought to mediate some of the prosocial and empathogenic dimensions of psychedelic therapy — trust in the therapist, willingness to open up, emotional warmth. If psilocybin (the most clinically studied psychedelic) doesn't robustly elevate oxytocin while mescaline and LSD do, this might matter for therapeutic mechanism and could influence which compound is most appropriate for different therapeutic contexts (e.g., disorders where social connection is central, like PTSD or addiction). However, this finding should be replicated before strong conclusions are drawn.

### Duration as a Practical Therapeutic Variable

An 11-hour mescaline session versus a 5-hour psilocybin session has real practical implications for therapy: therapist time, patient fatigue, clinic throughput, and the subjective challenge of sustaining a difficult psychedelic experience. Even though the peak intensity is equivalent, duration itself can shape the therapeutic arc.

## 6. Limitations & Open Questions

**The mescaline dose issue is a real limitation.** The switch from 300 to 500 mg was not randomized or blinded — the first 16 participants knew they were getting 300 mg. This breaks the clean within-subjects crossover design for the mescaline comparison and introduces potential expectancy effects.

**Healthy, experienced participants only.** The vast majority of participants had prior psychedelic experience, and the study excluded anyone with personal or family psychiatric history. This means the findings may not generalize to the patient populations most likely to receive these substances therapeutically.

**Psychometric insensitivity.** The 5D-ASC and MEQ are broad instruments. Subtle qualitative differences in the phenomenological character of the three substances — differences that experienced users sometimes report — may simply not be captured by these scales. The null result on subjective differences does not prove the experiences are truly identical; it proves they are not distinguishable by these particular instruments.

**BDNF measurement.** Using plasma instead of serum samples may systematically underestimate BDNF, making the null finding difficult to interpret. Prior work at higher LSD doses (200 µg) did find BDNF elevation, suggesting the signal exists but may require different conditions to detect.

**Mescaline's broader receptor profile was not directly probed.** The paper demonstrates equivalence at the behavioral level but cannot determine whether mescaline's additional activity at adrenergic and 5-HT1A receptors contributes anything to the subjective experience — only that any such contribution is not detectable with current instruments.

## 7. Key Terms & Concepts

**5-HT2A receptor:** A G protein-coupled receptor for serotonin found in high density in the prefrontal cortex. Agonism at this receptor is the shared mechanism responsible for psychedelic effects across structurally diverse compounds; it is the molecular target of your BioChemCore protein simulation work.

**Pharmacokinetics (PK):** How the body processes a drug over time — absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion. Key parameters here include Tmax (time to peak plasma concentration), Cmax (peak concentration), and t1/2 (elimination half-life, the time for plasma concentration to fall by half).

**Tmax:** The time after dosing at which plasma drug concentration reaches its maximum. Mescaline's longer Tmax (~2.3 h) compared to LSD (~1.4 h) is the main pharmacokinetic explanation for why mescaline's effects last longer.

**Psilocin:** The biologically active metabolite of psilocybin. Psilocybin itself is pharmacologically inert — it is rapidly dephosphorylated in the gut and liver to psilocin, which then crosses the blood-brain barrier and acts on 5-HT2A receptors. The paper measures psilocin plasma levels, not psilocybin.

**Crossover design:** An experimental design where each participant receives all conditions (rather than being assigned to just one). This eliminates individual differences as a confounding factor, dramatically increasing statistical power for the same sample size.

**Oxytocin:** A neuropeptide produced in the hypothalamus and released into circulation. Associated with social bonding, trust, and empathy. Proposed as one mediator of psychedelic-assisted therapy's prosocial therapeutic effects.

**BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor):** A protein that promotes neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, and the growth of new synaptic connections. Elevated BDNF has been proposed as one mechanism by which psychedelics produce lasting antidepressant or neuroplastic effects, though this paper found no acute elevation by any of the three substances.
