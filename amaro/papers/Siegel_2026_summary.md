# Summary: The Science of Psychedelic Medicine

**Citation:** Siegel, J.S., Liston, C., Nicol, G.E., Carhart-Harris, R.L., & Bogenschutz, M.P. (2026). The science of psychedelic medicine. *Nature Medicine*, 32, 449–462. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-025-04194-5

---

## One-Sentence Takeaway

Classic psychedelics — which act primarily at the serotonin 5-HT2A receptor — produce therapeutic benefit through two complementary biological mechanisms: acute neural desynchronization that disrupts rigid brain patterns, and a subsequent window of heightened neuroplasticity that allows lasting psychological and behavioral change.

---

## Background & Motivation

For most of the twentieth century, psychedelic compounds like psilocybin (the active ingredient in "magic mushrooms") and LSD sat at the fringes of psychiatry, viewed more as counterculture artifacts than legitimate medicines. That has changed dramatically. Over the past decade, rigorous clinical trials have demonstrated rapid and sustained therapeutic effects for depression, anxiety, PTSD, and addiction — often after just one or two sessions. This has pushed psychedelics to the forefront of translational neuroscience.

The core scientific puzzle driving this paper is mechanistic: *how* do these drugs produce lasting psychological change? Two broad hypotheses have circulated. The first focuses on the acute subjective experience — the ego dissolution, visual hallucinations, and emotional intensity that characterize a psychedelic trip — as the primary mediator of benefit. The second focuses on biological mechanisms: that psychedelics alter synaptic structure, gene expression, and neural circuit dynamics in ways that directly drive therapeutic outcomes regardless of (or in addition to) the subjective experience.

This review synthesizes the evidence across both frameworks, from single-neuron recordings up to whole-brain imaging and clinical trials. It is particularly timely because Phase 3 trials are currently underway for psilocybin in depression and LSD for anxiety, and because a new class of non-hallucinogenic psychedelic analogs (NHPAs) has emerged as a tool to test whether the subjective experience is actually necessary.

**Course connection:** The 5-HT2A receptor is the central protagonist of this paper. As a post-synaptic CNS membrane protein, it is exactly the type of protein the BioChemCore program is built around. Everything this paper describes — receptor density, agonist binding, G-protein signaling cascades, and downstream effects on dendritic morphology — flows from molecular-level events at that receptor. Understanding the biology here gives direct motivation for simulating 5-HT2A in a membrane environment.

## Approach & Methods

This is a comprehensive **review article** published in *Nature Medicine*, not a single experimental study. The authors — a team of psychiatrists and neuroscientists from NYU, Weill Cornell, Washington University, and UCSF/Imperial College London — synthesize hundreds of primary studies across three levels of analysis:

- **Cellular and molecular:** studies of receptor pharmacology, gene expression, BDNF signaling, dendritic spine morphology, and synaptic density
- **Systems neuroscience:** EEG, fMRI, and MEG studies of whole-brain network dynamics during and after psychedelic administration
- **Clinical:** randomized controlled trials and Phase 2/3 studies across neuropsychiatric indications

The authors explicitly organize their synthesis around two mechanistic frameworks — acute desynchronization and subacute neuroplasticity — and then evaluate how clinical evidence fits (or doesn't fit) within those frameworks. They also audit the active clinical trial landscape using ClinicalTrials.gov data as of December 2025, identifying 56 active trials for classic psychedelics and 14 for entactogens like MDMA.

## Key Findings

### 1. Classic psychedelics act primarily at the 5-HT2A receptor

The foundational pharmacology: classic psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD, DMT, mescaline, ayahuasca) are 5-HT2A agonists. The 5-HT2A receptor is expressed most densely in the association cortex and in the default mode network (DMN) — the brain regions that encode our sense of self, time, and introspection. This anatomical distribution explains why 5-HT2A activation has such profound effects on conscious experience. Other drug classes — dissociative anesthetics like ketamine (NMDA receptor antagonists) and entactogens like MDMA (monoamine release agents) — produce superficially similar altered states through entirely different receptor targets (Table 1 in the paper is a useful taxonomy of these drug classes).

### 2. Acute desynchronization: psychedelics break the brain's predictive rhythms

Under normal conditions, cortical neurons fire in synchronized, nested rhythms that support what the brain does most of the time: maintain a predictive model of reality. This synchronized activity is the neural substrate for habitual patterns of thought, perception, and behavior.

Activating 5-HT2A receptors on pyramidal neurons disrupts this synchrony. At the microscale, 5-HT2A activation decouples single-unit neuronal spiking from the local field potential — neurons become less likely to fire at the "expected" phase of the local rhythm and more likely to fire at random moments. At the macroscale, EEG and fMRI show a global reduction in synchronized oscillations and a corresponding increase in **entropy** — a measure of disorder or randomness in neural signals. This desynchronization is strongest in the DMN, which correlates with the subjective experience of ego dissolution.

The theoretical framing here draws on Bayesian brain models: by suppressing the synchronized activity that maintains prior beliefs, psychedelics may temporarily make the brain more open to new information and patterns. This is described as an increase in "changeability" — not just noise, but a state of genuine neural flexibility.

### 3. Subacute neuroplasticity: a window for lasting change

The desynchronization itself resolves as the drug clears. But animal and human data suggest that the disruption triggers a cascade of molecular events that outlast the acute drug effects by days to weeks. This is the **neuroplasticity** piece.

The mechanism parallels what has been established for ketamine. Chronic psychological stress suppresses brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) — a protein critical for maintaining dendritic spines (the physical sites of synaptic connections) in the prefrontal cortex and hippocampus. This suppression causes cortical atrophy and is a leading molecular model of depression. Antidepressants, including SSRIs, slowly reverse this, but it takes weeks. Ketamine does it in hours. Psilocybin and LSD appear to activate the same pathway: they rapidly upregulate plasticity-related genes in the hippocampus and frontal cortex, activate the TrkB receptor (BDNF's primary receptor), and produce rapid dendritic spine growth (synaptogenesis) that is measurable within 24 hours and can persist for more than a month.

This means the therapeutic action of psychedelics may not require being "tripping" — it may actually occur *after* the experience, as the brain uses its heightened plasticity to consolidate new patterns of thought and behavior. This is why the timing of psychotherapy and "integration" sessions (where patients process their experiences with a clinician) matters so much.

### 4. Network-specific effects map onto phenomenology

The desynchronization is not uniform across the brain. Psychedelics preferentially disrupt activity in 5-HT2A-rich areas: the DMN (correlating with ego dissolution and altered time perception) and the visual cortex (correlating with visual hallucinations). This modular disruption supports the idea that the specific phenomenology of a psychedelic experience — *which* aspects of consciousness are altered — directly reflects the anatomical distribution of the 5-HT2A receptor.

### 5. Strong clinical evidence for mood disorders; more limited elsewhere

The strongest clinical data comes from depression and anxiety. Psilocybin has been compared head-to-head with the SSRI escitalopram in depression (N = 59), showing comparable antidepressant efficacy but with psilocybin being clearly superior for anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure) and emotional engagement. A larger trial (N = 233) replicated this. Multiple Phase 2 randomized controlled trials have earned psilocybin the FDA's Breakthrough Therapy designation for treatment-resistant depression. LSD has shown significant anxiety reduction in two recent placebo-controlled trials.

For PTSD, MDMA-assisted therapy produced large and durable reductions in symptoms in Phase 3 trials, but the FDA declined to approve it over concerns about trial design integrity and blinding. The use of classic psychedelics for PTSD has received less clinical focus to date.

For addiction, evidence is promising but less consistent — studies of psilocybin for alcohol use disorder have shown conflicting results depending on sample size and design.

For pain, the preclinical data is mixed (some studies find no analgesic effect of psilocybin in rodent models, others find reduced hypersensitivity), though multiple NIH institutions have funded ongoing exploration.

### 6. Non-hallucinogenic psychedelic analogs (NHPAs) as scientific tools

A key controversy in the field is whether the subjective experience — the "trip" — is *necessary* for therapeutic benefit. If it is, then the drug and its experiential effects are inseparable and psychedelics require intensive psychological support infrastructure. If it is not, then simpler, non-hallucinogenic analogs could deliver the same neuroplastic benefits without requiring hours of supervised sessions.

NHPAs like 2-Br-LSD, AAZ-A-154, and tabernanthalog engage the 5-HT2A receptor in a **biased** or **functionally selective** manner — they activate the downstream signaling pathways linked to neuroplasticity (BDNF/TrkB, dendritic growth) while sparing the G-protein signaling pathways that drive hallucinations. In rodents, these compounds show the head-twitch response associated with 5-HT2A activation but not full hallucinogenic behavioral correlates. Whether this translates to humans remains genuinely uncertain and is one of the major open questions in the field.

### 7. Expectancy and unblinding are serious methodological problems

More than 90% of participants in psychedelic trials can correctly identify whether they received an active drug. This is called "functional unblinding," and it substantially complicates interpretation: positive expectancy effects could inflate apparent treatment effects. A recent meta-analysis suggests that this explains part (but not all) of the apparent drug-placebo difference. The FDA has acknowledged this and encouraged trials comparing psychedelics against other psychoactive agents (such as cannabis) to improve blinding quality.

## Significance & Implications

This paper is significant on several levels. It represents one of the most comprehensive attempts to build a unified mechanistic account of psychedelic medicine — one that connects receptor pharmacology to neural dynamics to clinical outcomes. The two-framework model (desynchronization + neuroplasticity) is a genuine conceptual advance over earlier accounts that focused exclusively on subjective experience or exclusively on receptor binding.

For the BioChemCore program, this paper provides direct biological motivation for studying the 5-HT2A receptor at the molecular level. The paper makes clear that:

- The **density and distribution** of 5-HT2A in the DMN and association cortex is what determines the phenomenological profile of psychedelics
- The **specific signaling pathways activated** downstream of 5-HT2A (G-protein vs. beta-arrestin vs. TrkB) determine whether a compound is hallucinogenic or just neuroplastic
- **Functional selectivity** (biased agonism) at 5-HT2A is a central design principle for next-generation therapeutics — making the molecular details of how agonists engage the receptor's active site directly therapeutically relevant

The broader implication is a paradigm shift for psychiatry. Current psychiatric drugs (SSRIs, antipsychotics) modulate neurotransmitter levels chronically, requiring daily dosing. Psychedelics appear to act as one-time catalysts that trigger a window of neuroplasticity — a fundamentally different treatment paradigm that blurs the boundary between pharmacology, psychology, and experience.

---

## Limitations & Open Questions

The authors are admirably direct about what the field does not yet know.

**Mechanistic gaps:** The causal chain from acute 5-HT2A activation to desynchronization to neuroplasticity to clinical benefit has not been fully established in humans. The link between acute desynchronization and subsequent plasticity is inferred largely from animal models and needs direct human validation.

**The subjective experience question is unresolved:** The NHPA data from rodents is suggestive, but rodents cannot report on subjective experience, and the head-twitch assay used as a proxy for hallucinogenic effects is imperfect. Whether NHPAs engage the same therapeutic mechanisms in humans is genuinely unknown.

**Blinding and expectancy:** The functional unblinding problem is not solved and probably cannot be fully solved with existing trial designs. This makes it difficult to determine how much of the clinical effect is pharmacological versus contextual/psychological.

**Microdosing:** The paper notes real cardiovascular risk concerns with chronic low-dose psychedelic use — particularly chronic 5-HT2B receptor stimulation, which can cause cardiac valvulopathy (a mechanism well-established with drugs like fenfluramine). This risk has not been adequately characterized in humans.

**Hallucinogen persisting perception disorder (HPPD):** The mechanism is unclear, and a broader range of persistent perceptual and cognitive disturbances after psychedelic use is underappreciated in clinical risk discussions.

**Scalability:** Psychedelic-assisted therapy as currently practiced requires multiple hours of clinician time per patient. Group therapy models and digital integration tools are being explored, but the infrastructure challenge is real and unsolved.

## Key Terms & Concepts

**5-HT2A receptor:** A G-protein coupled receptor (GPCR) for serotonin, expressed most densely in cortical pyramidal neurons of the association cortex and default mode network. The primary molecular target of classic psychedelics like psilocybin and LSD. Its activation is what triggers both the acute psychedelic state and the downstream neuroplastic cascade.

**Default Mode Network (DMN):** A large-scale brain network active during self-referential thinking, mind-wandering, and social cognition. Encodes our sense of self, autobiographical memory, and time perception. Preferentially disrupted by psychedelics due to its high 5-HT2A density — this is the neural correlate of ego dissolution.

**Neural entropy / desynchronization:** The increase in disorder or randomness in brain electrical signals during a psychedelic state. Reflects a breakdown of the normally tight synchronization of cortical oscillations. Conceptually, a shift from a brain locked into predictive, habitual patterns toward a more flexible and exploratory processing mode.

**Neuroplasticity (in this context):** The brain's capacity to form new synaptic connections and remodel existing circuits. Psychedelics appear to trigger rapid increases in neuroplasticity — measurable as dendritic spine growth, synaptogenesis, and upregulation of plasticity-related genes — that outlast the acute drug effects and may underlie lasting therapeutic benefit.

**BDNF / TrkB signaling:** Brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) is a protein that promotes neuronal survival, dendritic growth, and synaptic formation. It signals through its receptor TrkB (tropomyosin receptor kinase B). The BDNF/TrkB pathway is suppressed by chronic stress and depression, and is rapidly activated by ketamine and psychedelics — producing the rapid antidepressant-like structural changes in the prefrontal cortex and hippocampus.

**Biased agonism / functional selectivity:** A property of some receptor ligands where they activate only a subset of the downstream signaling pathways that a receptor can couple to. For 5-HT2A, NHPAs appear to selectively activate pathways linked to neuroplasticity (e.g., TrkB, beta-arrestin) while sparing pathways linked to hallucinations (e.g., Gq/phospholipase C). This is the molecular rationale for developing non-hallucinogenic psychedelic analogs.

**Integration (in psychedelic therapy):** The period after a psychedelic session during which patients work with a therapist to make sense of their experience and consolidate new insights into lasting behavioral change. Theoretically corresponds to the subacute neuroplasticity window — a time when the brain is more malleable and new patterns of thought can be more readily established.
