# Summary: Calderon et al. (1995) — Lipid Composition of Neuronal Cell Bodies and Neurites from Cultured Dorsal Root Ganglia

**Citation:** Calderon, R. O., Attema, B., & DeVries, G. H. (1995). Lipid composition of neuronal cell bodies and neurites from cultured dorsal root ganglia. *Journal of Neurochemistry, 64*(1), 424–429.

---

## 1. One-Sentence Takeaway

Neuronal cell bodies and their neurite extensions have distinctly different lipid compositions — neurites are enriched in cholesterol, sphingomyelin, complex gangliosides, and phosphatidylserine relative to cell bodies — indicating that neurons actively sort specific lipids to functionally distinct membrane compartments.

---

## 2. Background & Motivation

Neurons are architecturally unusual cells. Unlike most cells, which are roughly spherical or blobby, a neuron has a distinct cell body (called the soma or perikaryon) and long thin extensions called neurites — the axons and dendrites that form connections with other cells. These two compartments do very different things. The soma handles protein synthesis, houses the nucleus and organelles like the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and Golgi apparatus, and is the metabolic hub. The neurites, on the other hand, are the communication cables: they send and receive signals, and in peripheral sensory neurons, they extend all the way from your spine to your fingertips.

Because the soma and neurites have such different functional roles, there was good reason to suspect their membranes might have different lipid compositions. The lipid bilayer is not a passive backdrop — its composition directly determines how fluid or rigid the membrane is, how proteins are organized within it, and what signals the cell surface can display. But at the time of this study, almost nothing was known about how lipid composition differs between these two compartments in peripheral neurons.

The authors used dorsal root ganglia (DRG) as their model system. DRG are clusters of sensory neuron cell bodies found alongside the spinal cord; they relay touch, pain, and temperature signals from the body to the brain. DRG neurons are particularly useful for this kind of study because when grown in culture, they can be maintained free of contaminating glial cells (Schwann cells and fibroblasts) with antimitotic treatment. Crucially, after three weeks in culture, DRG neurons develop a dense halo of neurites that can be physically separated from the clustered cell bodies under a dissecting microscope — giving you two biochemically distinct fractions to analyze.

The broader motivation was to establish a lipid composition baseline for DRG cells before studying how neuron-Schwann cell interactions change membrane lipid organization. Understanding the resting lipid composition is a prerequisite for understanding how it gets modulated.

---

## 3. Approach & Methods

The experimental design is elegant in its simplicity: grow DRG neurons in culture, physically separate the cell bodies from the neurites, extract lipids from both fractions, and analyze them quantitatively.

**Cell preparation.** DRG cells were dissected from embryonic day 15 rat pups and plated on collagen-coated dishes. Antimitotics (uridine/fluorodeoxyuridine) were added to eliminate dividing Schwann cells and fibroblasts, leaving essentially pure neuronal cultures. After three weeks, neurites formed a visible halo around clustered cell bodies.

**Physical separation.** This is the key methodological step. The clustered neuronal somata were removed from the dish manually with forceps under a dissecting microscope. The remaining neurite fraction was then scraped off with a rubber policeman. Both fractions were washed, pelleted by centrifugation, and sonicated for lipid extraction.

**Lipid extraction and fractionation.** Total lipids were extracted using chloroform/methanol/water (the standard Folch-type extraction approach). The extract was then fractionated on a DEAE-Sephadex column into two pools: neutral lipids (which includes sphingomyelin, phosphatidylcholine, phosphatidylethanolamine, cholesterol, cerebrosides) and acidic lipids (which includes phosphatidylserine, phosphatidylinositol, sulfatides, free fatty acids, and gangliosides). This separation takes advantage of the charge on acidic lipid headgroups.

**Analysis.** Individual lipid species were resolved and quantified by high-performance thin-layer chromatography (HPTLC) with densitometry. This technique separates lipids by their polarity on a silica gel plate, then visualizes them by charring with cupric acetate/phosphoric acid and scanning the resulting bands. Gangliosides were analyzed separately using a resorcinol-HCl stain, which is specific for sialic acid-containing glycolipids.

**Why this approach works.** The physical separation method is simple and avoids cell disruption artifacts. The HPTLC approach with densitometry was well-validated for brain lipid analysis at this time. The use of antimitotics to purify the neuronal culture is critical — contaminating Schwann cells would have their own distinct lipid profile and would confound any comparison between soma and neurite fractions.

## 4. Key Findings

### Neurites are much leaner in total lipid

The most striking overall difference is that cell bodies contain roughly 2.4 times more lipid per unit protein than neurites (0.36 vs. 0.15 µg lipid/µg protein). This makes biochemical sense: the soma houses the ER and Golgi, which are lipid-rich biosynthetic organelles with their own membranes. The neurites are mostly plasma membrane and cytoskeleton.

### Neurites have a higher cholesterol-to-phospholipid ratio

The cholesterol/phospholipid molar ratio was 0.52 in cell bodies but 0.74 in neurites. This is a meaningful difference. Cholesterol inserts between phospholipid tails and acts as a "membrane fluidizer" at low temperatures but a "membrane rigidifier" at physiological temperatures — it stabilizes the liquid-ordered phase. A higher cholesterol ratio suggests the neuritic membrane is more tightly packed and ordered than the somatic membrane.

This also makes biological sense because cholesterol is known to be enriched in the plasma membrane compared to the ER. Since neurites are nearly all plasma membrane (no ER), a higher C/phospholipid ratio is consistent with less ER membrane diluting the cholesterol-rich plasma membrane pool.

### Neurites are enriched in sphingomyelin and phosphatidylserine

Among phospholipid classes, the overall proportions of phosphatidylcholine (PC) and phosphatidylethanolamine (PE) are similar between the two compartments. But neurites contain 61% more sphingomyelin (Sph) than cell bodies (4.47% vs. 2.77% of total lipids), and 2.3-fold more phosphatidylserine (PS) (6.23% vs. 3.08%).

Sphingomyelin is significant because it interacts strongly with cholesterol — they co-partition into "lipid rafts," which are tightly packed, ordered microdomains in the plasma membrane that serve as platforms for signaling proteins. The co-enrichment of both Sph and cholesterol in neurites strongly implies the presence of these ordered microdomains in the neurite plasma membrane.

Phosphatidylserine is normally confined to the inner leaflet of the plasma membrane by a lipid flippase (sometimes called a "lipid pump"). When PS is flipped to the outer leaflet — something that happens during apoptosis or certain signaling events — it becomes a recognition signal for macrophages and affects cell-cell interactions. The elevated PS in neurites may thus play a role in regulating how neurites interact with nearby Schwann cells during development.

### Neurites are enriched in galactolipids

Cerebrosides (CB) and sulfatides (Sulf) — collectively called galactolipids — were both elevated in neurites (7.71% total galactolipids in neurites vs. 4.80% in cell bodies). The cerebroside/sulfatide molar ratio was the same in both compartments (~4.6:1), suggesting the relative proportions are fixed but the absolute amount is higher in neurites. Galactolipids are characteristic components of myelin and nervous system membranes.

### Ganglioside composition is qualitatively different, not just quantitatively different

This is arguably the most striking finding in the paper. Gangliosides are complex glycolipids that carry one or more sialic acid residues on their sugar headgroups. They are named with a G (for ganglioside), followed by letters and numbers indicating the number of sialic acids and the structure (GM = mono-sialo, GD = di-sialo, GT = tri-sialo, GQ = quad-sialo).

Neurites contain roughly twice as much total ganglioside as cell bodies (0.186 vs. 0.088 µg/µg lipid). But more importantly, the *identity* of the gangliosides differs between compartments:

- **Cell bodies** contain the full spectrum: simple gangliosides GM3, GM2, and GM1 are present alongside more complex ones (GD1a, GD1b, GT1b, GQ1b) and GD3.
- **Neurites** contain NO monosialogangliosides (GM1, GM2, GM3 are completely absent). They contain only the more complex species: GQ1b, GT1b, GD1b, GD1a, and GD3.

This qualitative difference suggests active sorting — simpler gangliosides synthesized in the soma are either retained there or degraded before reaching the neurite plasma membrane, while more complex gangliosides are preferentially trafficked outward. The authors propose this reflects a functional barrier between the soma and neurite plasma membranes (consistent with work by Kobayashi et al. 1992 showing a lipid diffusion barrier at the axon initial segment).

GD3 shows an interesting pattern: it is present in both compartments, but is actually *decreased* in neurites (6.42% vs. 13.10% of total sialic acid in neurites vs. soma). Its enrichment in the soma after neurite outgrowth may reflect a distinct function in the perikaryon.

## 5. Significance & Implications

### Direct relevance to your MD simulations and membrane building

This paper is a primary reference for neuronal membrane lipid composition. When you build a post-synaptic membrane in CHARMM-GUI for your BioChemCore simulations, you need to specify what lipids go in the bilayer and in what proportions. This paper gives you empirically measured ratios for neuronal compartments. The key takeaway for membrane building is that neurite plasma membranes (which approximate the post-synaptic membrane environment of CNS dendritic spines) are characterized by:

- High cholesterol (~22% of dry weight, C/phospholipid ratio ~0.74)
- Elevated sphingomyelin (consistent with lipid raft formation)
- PC and PE as the dominant phospholipids
- Elevated phosphatidylserine
- Galactolipids (cerebrosides and sulfatides in ~4.6:1 ratio)
- Complex gangliosides only (no GM-series)

This is the lipid environment in which your post-synaptic membrane proteins actually live. Getting the lipid composition right matters because MD simulations are extremely sensitive to the physical properties of the bilayer — membrane fluidity, thickness, and lateral pressure profile all affect protein conformation and dynamics.

### Lipid sorting and membrane domain formation

The paper provides early direct evidence that neurons actively sort lipids between compartments. The co-enrichment of cholesterol, sphingomyelin, and glycosphingolipids in neurites is chemically significant: these three classes of lipid are precisely the ones known to form liquid-ordered (Lo) domains — lipid rafts. This suggests that neuritic membranes are more raft-like and less fluid than somatic membranes. In the context of post-synaptic membrane biology, raft microdomains are important for organizing neurotransmitter receptors and signal transduction machinery.

### Phosphatidylserine and membrane asymmetry

The elevated PS in neurites is functionally interesting. Because PS is normally restricted to the inner leaflet, a higher PS content means more inner-leaflet PS available as a signaling lipid. PS can activate protein kinase C, interact with cytoskeletal proteins like spectrin, and regulate membrane curvature. Any disruption of PS asymmetry in the neurite membrane would change how the neurite surface is recognized by surrounding cells — relevant to Schwann cell interactions and axon-glial signaling.

### Neurochemistry and the PNS lipid landscape

Before this paper, essentially nothing was known about how lipid composition differs between the soma and neurite compartments of peripheral neurons. The data established that these are not just quantitatively different but qualitatively different membranes — they have distinct identities. This has downstream implications for understanding how axonal signals regulate Schwann cell development and myelination.

## 6. Limitations & Open Questions

**Culture vs. in vivo.** DRG neurons grown in culture for three weeks are not identical to neurons in an intact animal. Culturing conditions, the absence of Schwann cell contact, and the embryonic origin of the cells all potentially alter lipid composition compared to adult DRG in vivo. The paper acknowledges the intent to use this as a baseline before adding Schwann cells to the coculture.

**Physical separation is imperfect.** The manual separation of soma clusters from neurites, while visually guided, almost certainly results in some cross-contamination. The soma fraction may contain some short neurite stubs; the neurite fraction may contain fragments of soma membrane that were dragged away. The paper doesn't quantify the purity of the two fractions.

**No fatty acid composition data.** The paper tells us which phospholipid headgroups are present but says nothing about the fatty acid chains attached to them. The saturation level and chain length of fatty acids in the phospholipids profoundly affect membrane fluidity — a PC with two saturated chains is very different from a PC with an unsaturated arachidonic acid tail. For a complete membrane composition to use in MD simulations, you'd need to pair this headgroup data with fatty acid composition data from other sources (e.g., Harayama & Riezman 2018).

**Ganglioside sorting mechanism is unresolved.** The paper describes the result — simpler gangliosides are absent from neurites — but cannot explain the mechanism. Is it active retrograde transport back to the soma? A sorting step in the Golgi that excludes simpler species from neurite-destined vesicles? Degradation by glycosidases in the neurite? These questions were open in 1995 and remain partially open.

**DRG is PNS, not CNS.** The program focuses on post-synaptic CNS membrane proteins. Dorsal root ganglia are peripheral nervous system neurons. While the basic lipid biology is likely transferable, CNS neurons and PNS neurons are not identical — CNS neurons interact with oligodendrocytes rather than Schwann cells, and their myelin chemistry is somewhat different. Use this paper as a strong guide for general neuronal membrane composition, but note the distinction.

## 7. Key Terms & Concepts

**Ganglioside.** A complex glycolipid found in cell membranes, especially in neurons, that has a ceramide lipid anchor embedded in the membrane and one or more sialic acid residues on its sugar headgroup facing extracellularly; they function in cell signaling, cell recognition, and modulating receptor activity.

**Sphingomyelin (Sph).** A sphingolipid phospholipid made of a ceramide backbone (sphingosine + fatty acid) linked to a phosphocholine headgroup; it tends to form tightly packed, ordered domains in membranes and has a strong affinity for cholesterol.

**Cholesterol/phospholipid molar ratio.** A measure of how much cholesterol is present relative to the bulk of the membrane bilayer; a higher ratio generally indicates a more ordered, less fluid membrane because cholesterol fills gaps between tightly packed phospholipid tails.

**Lipid raft.** A nanoscale, dynamic microdomain in the plasma membrane enriched in cholesterol, sphingomyelin, and glycosphingolipids that exists in a liquid-ordered phase and acts as an organizational platform for signaling proteins and membrane receptors.

**Phosphatidylserine (PS).** An anionic (negatively charged) phospholipid normally confined to the inner leaflet of the plasma membrane by a flippase enzyme; its translocation to the outer leaflet is a signal for apoptosis or cell-cell recognition.

**Galactolipids (cerebrosides and sulfatides).** Glycosphingolipids where a galactose (or sulfated galactose) is attached to ceramide; they are highly enriched in myelin and nervous system membranes and contribute to membrane rigidity.

**Liquid-ordered (Lo) phase.** A physical state of membrane lipids in which the acyl chains are tightly packed (ordered) but the lipids still have lateral mobility (liquid); this phase is promoted by cholesterol and sphingomyelin and is distinct from both the disordered fluid phase and the rigid gel phase.
