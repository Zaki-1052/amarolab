# Carhart-Harris et al. (2018) — Psilocybin with Psychological Support for Treatment-Resistant Depression: Six-Month Follow-Up

**Citation:** Carhart-Harris, R. L., Bolstridge, M., Day, C. M. J., Rucker, J., Watts, R., Erritzoe, D. E., Kaelen, M., Giribaldi, B., Bloomfield, M., Pilling, S., Rickard, J. A., Forbes, B., Feilding, A., Taylor, D., Curran, H. V., & Nutt, D. J. (2018). Psilocybin with psychological support for treatment-resistant depression: six-month follow-up. *Psychopharmacology, 235*(3), 399–408. https://doi.org/10.1007/s00213-017-4771-x

---

## One-Sentence Takeaway

Twenty patients with treatment-resistant depression who had failed multiple antidepressants received just two oral doses of psilocybin in a supportive setting, and the majority showed marked, sustained reductions in depressive symptoms that persisted for up to six months — with the quality of the acute psychedelic experience predicting how well they responded.

---

## Background & Motivation

Major depressive disorder (MDD) is one of the most debilitating conditions worldwide, and a significant proportion of patients are considered "treatment-resistant" — meaning their depression does not improve despite adequate trials of at least two pharmacologically distinct antidepressants. These patients represent a population for whom current medicine has very little to offer. Standard medications like SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) essentially force more serotonin to remain in the synaptic cleft by blocking reuptake transporters, but for treatment-resistant patients, this approach has already failed repeatedly.

Psilocybin is a naturally occurring compound found in certain mushrooms. Once ingested, it is rapidly dephosphorylated in the body to its active form, psilocin, which acts as a potent agonist at the serotonin 2A receptor (5-HT2AR) — the same receptor that is central to the pharmacology of all classical psychedelics. Importantly, this receptor is highly expressed in the prefrontal cortex and other limbic regions involved in mood regulation, which is why 5-HT2AR agonism is thought to be the gateway through which psilocybin produces both its perceptual effects and its putative antidepressant action.

This paper is a six-month follow-up to an earlier feasibility study (Carhart-Harris et al. 2016) that had reported positive outcomes at three months in 12 patients. The updated study expanded the cohort to 20 patients and extended the follow-up period to six months, aiming to assess whether early antidepressant effects were durable and to gather more data on safety and the predictors of therapeutic response.

This paper situates that receptor as a real-world therapeutic target — one whose activation by psilocybin is proposed to trigger a cascade of plasticity-promoting intracellular signaling. ## Approach & Methods

### Patient Population

The study recruited adults diagnosed with severe, unipolar, treatment-resistant major depression — specifically, individuals who had failed at least two courses of pharmacologically distinct antidepressants of adequate dose and duration (minimum six weeks) within the current episode. Patients with any personal or immediate family history of psychotic disorders were excluded, given the known risk of psychedelics precipitating psychosis in vulnerable individuals. The final cohort of 20 patients (6 female, 14 male) had a mean illness duration of 17.7 years and had tried a median of 4.6 different medications. These were genuinely refractory cases.

### Treatment Protocol

Eligible patients underwent a psychiatric screening visit, baseline MRI scan, and informed consent process. Before any drug was administered, patients engaged in psychological preparation — meeting the study therapists, building rapport, and learning what to expect from the psilocybin experience. This preparation phase was considered essential, not optional.

Treatment involved two oral dosing sessions separated by one week:
- **Session 1:** 10 mg psilocybin (a lower dose to acclimate the patient to the experience)
- **Session 2:** 25 mg psilocybin (the full therapeutic dose, given one week later)

During both sessions, patients reclined in a comfortable room with eye shades and curated music, accompanied by two trained therapists who provided support but did not direct or interpret the experience in real time. The day after the 25 mg session, patients attended a debriefing session (integration phase) where they could discuss the experience with therapists and begin making sense of it.

Patients were advised to taper off antidepressants before the trial to prevent pharmacological interference with psilocybin's effects, since chronic SSRI use can attenuate the subjective response to 5-HT2AR agonists.

### Outcome Measures

The primary outcome was the Quick Inventory of Depressive Symptomatology — Self-Rated 16-item version (QIDS-SR16), a validated self-report scale measuring depressive symptom severity. This was collected at 1, 2, 3, and 5 weeks, and 3 and 6 months post-treatment. Secondary measures included:

- **BDI** (Beck Depression Inventory) — another self-report depression scale
- **HAM-D** (Hamilton Depression Rating Scale) — clinician-administered depression measure
- **STAI** (State-Trait Anxiety Inventory) — anxiety symptoms
- **SHAPS** (Snaith-Hamilton Pleasure Scale) — anhedonia (the inability to feel pleasure, a core symptom of depression)
- **GAF** (Global Assessment of Functioning) — overall day-to-day function

The acute psychedelic experience itself was rated using the **11-Dimension Altered States of Consciousness questionnaire (11D-ASC)**, completed at the end of each dosing day. This 94-item scale captures dimensions including experience of unity, spiritual experience, blissful state, insightfulness, and complex imagery. The authors were specifically interested in whether the quality of the acute experience could predict longer-term outcomes.

### Statistical Analysis

Two-tailed paired t-tests were used to compare pre- versus post-treatment scores at each time point. A Bonferroni-corrected alpha of 0.0083 (0.05/6) was applied for the six QIDS-SR16 contrasts against baseline, to guard against false positives from multiple comparisons. Effect sizes were reported as Cohen's d.

## Key Findings

### Depressive Symptoms Dropped Markedly and Rapidly

All 19 completers showed some reduction in QIDS-SR16 depression scores at 1 week post-treatment. The maximum group-level effect occurred at 5 weeks post-treatment (Cohen's d = 2.3, which is a very large effect — for context, typical antidepressant trials in treatment-resistant patients often yield Cohen's d values of 0.3–0.5). Mean QIDS-SR16 scores fell from approximately 19 at baseline to around 9 at 5 weeks. Effects remained statistically significant at both 3 months (Cohen's d = 1.5) and 6 months (Cohen's d = 1.6), both p < 0.001.

Nine of nineteen patients met criteria for treatment response at 5 weeks (defined as a 50% or greater reduction from baseline QIDS), and four met criteria for remission (QIDS score below the clinical threshold). No patients sought conventional antidepressant treatment within 5 weeks of the psilocybin session — an important detail, because it means the observed improvements were not confounded by patients starting new medications in the early weeks.

### Effects Were Consistent Across Multiple Instruments

The finding was not an artifact of using a single scale. BDI scores were significantly reduced at 1 week, 3 months, and 6 months post-treatment. HAM-D (clinician-rated) scores were significantly reduced at 1 week. Anxiety (STAI) was significantly reduced at 1 week, 3 months, and 6 months. Anhedonia (SHAPS) improved significantly at 1 week and 3 months. Importantly, Global Assessment of Functioning (GAF) scores increased significantly at 1 week — meaning patients were not just reporting feeling better subjectively, but functioning better in daily life. Suicidality items on the QIDS and HAM-D were also significantly reduced post-treatment.

Genital/sexual dysfunction items on the HAM-D were significantly reduced at 1 week — the opposite of the sexual side effects commonly associated with SSRIs, which is notable and clinically relevant.

### The Quality of the Psychedelic Experience Predicted Outcomes

The 11D-ASC questionnaire revealed that higher scores on the 25 mg session for "experience of unity," "spiritual experience," "blissful state," and "insightfulness" were significantly greater after 25 mg than 10 mg psilocybin. These four dimensions were highly correlated with each other (r > 0.92 for all pairwise comparisons), so the authors combined them into a single factor they called "USB" (Unity, Spiritual experience, Blissful state).

Critically, higher USB scores during the 25 mg session predicted greater reductions in depression at 5 weeks (r = -0.49, p = 0.03). Separately, the "insightfulness" factor also significantly predicted depression improvement at 5 weeks (r = -0.57, p = 0.01). This is an important mechanistic hint: it suggests the therapeutic effect is not merely pharmacological but depends on the nature of the psychological experience the drug induces. The drug seems to work, at least in part, by creating conditions for meaningful psychological insight and reorganization.

### Safety Profile Was Acceptable

Treatment was generally well tolerated. There were no serious adverse events. The most common side effects were transient anxiety (lasting minutes, n = 15) and headaches lasting no more than 1–2 days (n = 8). Five patients reported transient nausea and three reported brief paranoia, but both resolved quickly in every case. There were no reports of flashbacks or persisting perceptual changes after the acute session ended.

One patient became uncommunicative during the peak of the 25 mg experience. He was not harmed, and later reported the experience had been "blissful but overwhelming." He subsequently dropped out of further follow-up assessment, though his 6-month QIDS score of 25 and BDI of 40 indicate he did not maintain the therapeutic benefit.

Fourteen patients reported autobiographical visions during their sessions. One patient reported a disturbing memory that he had not previously been conscious of, which subsequently led to transient worsening of symptoms at weeks 2–3 before he improved again. The study team responded with careful, non-judgmental listening — a reminder that the psychological support framework is not merely procedural but actively therapeutic.

### Attrition and Confounds at 3–6 Months

After the 3-month mark, some patients initiated new treatments. Six began new antidepressant courses and five started new psychotherapy between 3 and 6 months. Five patients also independently obtained psilocybin outside the study between the 3- and 6-month assessments. When those five are excluded from the 6-month analysis, the effect sizes actually remain similar (d = 1.7 at 3 months, d = 1.7 at 6 months), suggesting the primary signal was not driven by these post-study interventions. However, this confounding limits how cleanly one can attribute the 6-month outcomes to psilocybin alone.

## Significance & Implications

### A Proof-of-Concept for a Mechanistically Distinct Antidepressant Approach

This paper is significant not because it proves psilocybin works in a clinical sense — the open-label design and n=20 make that impossible — but because it extends and strengthens the proof-of-concept that a profoundly different mechanism of antidepressant action is achievable. Where SSRIs act chronically on serotonin availability over weeks or months to produce modest average effects, psilocybin produced rapid, large-magnitude antidepressant effects after just two sessions, and those effects were still detectable six months later.

The proposed mechanism, which the authors describe elsewhere in more detail, centers on 5-HT2AR-mediated induction of a transient state of heightened neuroplasticity. Activating the 5-HT2AR — especially the dense population of these receptors in cortical layer V pyramidal neurons — appears to trigger downstream signaling cascades (including BDNF/TrkB, mTOR, and AMPA receptor trafficking) that promote dendritic remodeling and synaptic growth. The finding that "insightfulness" during the acute experience predicts later remission fits this model: the drug may create a biologically and psychologically plastic window during which habitual, depressive cognitive patterns can be revised and new ones established.

### Relevance to 5-HT2AR Molecular Dynamics

For your BioChemCore work on the 5-HT2AR, this paper provides the clinical anchor for why the receptor matters. The key mechanistic insight is that 5-HT2AR agonism (not antagonism) is the relevant pharmacological action here — and yet the receptor is paradoxically also the target of many effective antidepressant augmentation strategies that work through antagonism. This duality, discussed in the paper, implies that the receptor can mediate antidepressant effects through at least two different conformational/signaling modes (a concept explored in detail in biased agonism literature, e.g. Jastrzębski 2025 and Wallach 2023 in your reading list). When you simulate the 5-HT2AR with psilocin bound versus in its unliganded or SSRI-modulated state, you are effectively modeling the structural basis of the therapeutic effect documented here.

### The Context-Dependence of the Therapeutic Outcome

The correlation between acute psychological experience quality and long-term antidepressant outcome is a striking translational finding. It implies that receptor pharmacology alone cannot explain the therapeutic effect — the subjective experience produced by receptor activation is itself therapeutically active. This has a structural/molecular correlate: different ligands at 5-HT2AR engage different downstream effector proteins (Gq vs. beta-arrestin, for example) and produce different conformational ensembles of the receptor, which your MD simulation can help characterize.

## Limitations & Open Questions

**Open-label design with no control condition.** This is the central limitation the authors explicitly acknowledge. There is no placebo group, no active comparator, and no blinding. The observed effects could be inflated by expectancy, the therapeutic alliance, the intensive clinical attention, or the novelty of the experience. The effect sizes are so large (Cohen's d = 2.3 at 5 weeks) that it seems unlikely expectancy alone explains them — but this cannot be ruled out without a proper randomized controlled trial (RCT).

**Small sample, limited demographic diversity.** Twenty patients, six female, predominantly white. Treatment-resistant depression may have modestly higher rates in women, so the predominantly male sample limits generalizability. The authors acknowledge this directly.

**Post-study contamination.** Between 3 and 6 months, patients began new medications and psychotherapy, and five obtained psilocybin independently. This makes attributing 6-month outcomes specifically to the study treatment untenable.

**Assessment of illness duration was self-reported.** Patients were asked how long their current episode had lasted. As the authors note, patients often reported the duration of their chronic illness rather than a discrete episode, which complicates the clinical picture.

**Psychological support was not standardized beyond core principles.** The "PSI" framework (Preparation, Support, Integration) is described but was not manualized at the time of the study, making replication of the exact psychological component difficult.

**Key open question:** What is the minimum effective dose? What is the optimal dosing interval? What role does the psychological support model play versus the pharmacology alone? Do the effects of psilocybin actually rival those of daily antidepressants over 6+ months, or does benefit attenuate further out? The authors explicitly call for a placebo-controlled RCT as the necessary next step.

## Key Terms & Concepts

**5-HT2AR (serotonin 2A receptor):** The primary molecular target of psilocybin's active metabolite, psilocin. It is a Gq-coupled GPCR highly expressed in the prefrontal cortex. Its agonism by classical psychedelics is responsible for both the perceptual and the putative neuroplastic effects of these compounds.

**Treatment-resistant depression (TRD):** Major depressive disorder that fails to respond to at least two adequate pharmacological treatments. These patients represent approximately 30% of all MDD cases and have extremely limited therapeutic options.

**QIDS-SR16 (Quick Inventory of Depressive Symptomatology, Self-Rated 16-item):** The primary outcome measure in this study. A validated self-report questionnaire where scores of 16 and above indicate severe depression; lower scores indicate symptom improvement.

**Cohen's d:** A standardized effect size measure expressing how many standard deviations the treatment group's mean differs from baseline. Values above 0.8 are considered "large"; the d = 2.3 seen at 5 weeks here is exceptionally large for a psychiatric intervention.

**11D-ASC (11-Dimension Altered States of Consciousness questionnaire):** A validated 94-item self-report scale used to characterize the subjective quality of altered states of consciousness. In this study, its "USB" (Unity, Spiritual experience, Blissful state) composite factor during the acute psilocybin experience predicted antidepressant outcomes five weeks later.

**Biased agonism / functional selectivity:** The concept that different ligands binding the same receptor can stabilize different receptor conformations, selectively activating some downstream signaling pathways over others. Relevant here because psilocybin and endogenous serotonin both activate 5-HT2AR but may do so in ways that differ at the level of Gq versus beta-arrestin recruitment — a distinction your MD simulation work can help illuminate.

**Open-label trial:** A clinical study in which both researchers and participants know what treatment is being administered. The opposite of a double-blind placebo-controlled trial. Results are considered preliminary and proof-of-concept rather than definitive evidence of efficacy.
