# Shapiro et al. (2003) — Aripiprazole, A Novel Atypical Antipsychotic Drug with a Unique and Robust Pharmacology

**Full citation:** Shapiro DA, Renock S, Arrington E, Chiodo LA, Liu L-X, Sibley DR, Roth BL, Mailman R. *Neuropsychopharmacology* (2003) **28**, 1400–1411.

---

## 1. One-Sentence Takeaway

Aripiprazole is not simply a D2 partial agonist as previously believed — it is a pharmacologically complex drug with functionally selective, cell-type-dependent actions at dopamine receptors and significant activity across a wide range of serotonin receptor subtypes, which together likely explain its unique clinical profile.

---

## 2. Background & Motivation

Schizophrenia affects more than 1% of the world's population and is one of the most costly chronic diseases in psychiatry. It is characterized by positive symptoms (delusions, hallucinations) and negative symptoms (social withdrawal, lack of motivation, cognitive impairment). For decades, treatment relied on "typical" antipsychotics like haloperidol and chlorpromazine, which work by blocking D2-dopamine receptors. These drugs were reasonably effective against positive symptoms in about 70–80% of patients, but they caused serious side effects — particularly extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) like Parkinsonism, and the long-term side effect tardive dyskinesia, which involves involuntary movements.

The next generation — "atypical" antipsychotics, starting with clozapine in the late 1980s — represented a major advance. Atypicals are defined by their relatively low affinity for D2-dopamine receptors combined with relatively high affinity for 5-HT2A serotonin receptors. This shifted the pharmacological target from dopamine alone toward a more balanced serotonin-dopamine interaction. Clozapine, the gold standard atypical, dramatically reduces both positive and negative symptoms with minimal EPS — but it carries risks of life-threatening agranulocytosis (a dangerous drop in white blood cells) and substantial weight gain.

Aripiprazole (OPC-14597) arrived in clinical practice with a proposed mechanism different from all prior atypicals: it was described as a high-affinity D2-dopamine receptor **partial agonist**. Rather than blocking dopamine receptors outright, it was thought to sit in a middle ground — weakly activating the receptor in dopamine-depleted brain regions while damping excess dopamine activity in overactive circuits. This "dopamine stabilization" concept was appealing, but the full molecular picture of how aripiprazole actually behaves across the brain's receptor landscape was incomplete.

This paper sets out to fill that gap. The authors — a multi-institution team including Bryan Roth's group at Case Western — conducted the most comprehensive pharmacological profiling of aripiprazole at that point, testing it against a large panel of GPCRs, ion channels, transporters, and kinases. Their goal was to understand not just where aripiprazole binds, but what it actually does when it gets there.

---

## 3. Approach & Methods

The study used three complementary experimental strategies that together capture both binding (affinity) and functional output (signaling).

**Radioligand binding assays** were the starting point. Using human cloned cDNAs from the NIMH Psychoactive Drug Screening Program (NIMH-PDSP), the authors screened aripiprazole at 10 µM across a broad panel of receptors, channels, and transporters. Where inhibition exceeded 50%, they followed up with Ki determinations across at least six concentrations of aripiprazole. The Ki value represents the concentration needed to displace half the radioligand — lower Ki means higher binding affinity. This gave the team a quantitative map of where aripiprazole sticks.

Binding tells you where a drug goes, but not what it does once it gets there. The functional assays addressed this.

**PI hydrolysis assays** measured activation of the Gq signaling pathway. When a Gq-coupled receptor (like 5-HT2A or 5-HT2C) is activated, it triggers phospholipase C, which cleaves a membrane lipid called phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate into inositol phosphates (IPs). The researchers radiolabeled inositol with [3H]-inositol and measured accumulation of [3H]-IPs as a readout of Gq signaling. This was done in multiple cell lines — C6-glioma cells expressing endogenous rat 5-HT2A, GF62 cells with rat 5-HT2A transfected in, HEK-293 cells transfected with 5-HT2B, and PO1C cells with rat 5-HT2C.

**cAMP assays** measured Gs and Gi pathway activity. Receptors like 5-HT1A and D2 signal through G proteins that either raise (Gs) or lower (Gi) intracellular cAMP. The authors used a competitive protein binding assay with [3H]-cAMP to quantify cAMP levels after aripiprazole treatment, either with or without forskolin (a direct activator of adenylyl cyclase). The difference in signal between drug-treated and vehicle-treated cells reveals agonist versus antagonist behavior.

**Electrophysiology** was used specifically for D2L, D3, and D4 receptor studies. MES-23.5 cells stably transfected with these receptor subtypes were patch-clamped in whole-cell configuration. The researchers measured outward K+ currents, which are one output of D2-family receptor activation. This system enabled real-time, single-cell resolution of receptor functional activity.

A critical methodological point the paper highlights: aripiprazole's affinity at D2 receptors is profoundly sensitive to sodium ion concentration. Assays run in sodium-containing buffers (physiological conditions) show dramatically lower affinity than those run in sodium-free buffers. This is because sodium ions decrease agonist affinities at D2-family receptors. This detail explains why earlier published Ki values for aripiprazole at D2 were all over the map — different labs used different assay buffers.

## 4. Key Findings

### Binding Profile: Broader Than Expected

Table 1 is the backbone of this paper. Aripiprazole had the highest affinity for **5-HT2B** receptors (Ki = 0.36 nM — subnanomolar), followed by hD2L (Ki = 0.74 nM), rD3 (Ki = 1.0 nM), and hD3 (Ki = 3.3 nM). These are exceptionally tight interactions.

At serotonin receptors, aripiprazole showed moderate-to-high affinity for 5-HT1A (Ki = 5.6 nM), 5-HT2A (Ki = 8.7 nM by the [3H]ketanserin assay), and 5-HT7 (Ki = 10.3 nM) — all in the single-digit nanomolar range. Notably, affinity for 5-HT2A was 10–100 fold lower than other atypical antipsychotics (except quetiapine), which challenges the simple narrative that 5-HT2A blockade is the central feature of atypicality.

Aripiprazole also bound alpha-1A adrenergic receptors (Ki = 25.7 nM) and histamine H1 receptors (Ki = 25.1 nM) with moderate affinity. The H1 affinity is clinically relevant — H1 antagonism is the strongest predictor of short-term weight gain with antipsychotics, and aripiprazole's relatively modest H1 affinity predicts minimal weight gain, which has borne out in clinical experience.

### Functional Effects at Serotonin Receptors: A Range of Actions

At **5-HT1A** receptors (CHO cells), aripiprazole was a low-potency partial agonist, inhibiting forskolin-stimulated cAMP with an EC50 of 329 nM — far less potent than serotonin itself (EC50 ~4.5 nM). Its intrinsic activity was also low.

At **5-HT2A** receptors, the picture was nuanced and cell-type dependent. In C6-glioma cells (which have low receptor reserve — meaning relatively few spare receptors), aripiprazole caused only minimal PI hydrolysis at the highest concentration tested (10 µM). In GF62 cells (higher receptor reserve), aripiprazole was a partial agonist with an EC50 of 48 nM but only 12.7% intrinsic activity relative to serotonin. The authors conclude that in vivo, aripiprazole likely behaves as a 5-HT2A **antagonist** because most brain regions lack substantial receptor reserve. This is important: the drug that might look like a partial agonist in a high-expression cell line may function as a net antagonist in the actual synapse.

At **5-HT2B** receptors, aripiprazole was an **inverse agonist** — it decreased basal PI hydrolysis from a spontaneous level of 61% down to 30% at an EC50 of 11 nM. Inverse agonism means the drug suppresses even the receptor's constitutive (ligand-independent) activity. This is clinically favorable: 5-HT2B agonism is associated with cardiac valvulopathy (as seen with fenfluramine), so inverse agonism predicts aripiprazole will not cause this side effect.

At **5-HT2C** receptors, aripiprazole was a partial agonist with notably high intrinsic activity (82% of serotonin) in PO1C cells expressing rat 5-HT2C-VGV. This 5-HT2C partial agonism may contribute to aripiprazole's favorable weight profile — 5-HT2C activation promotes satiety.

At **5-HT6** receptors, aripiprazole was an antagonist with no intrinsic activity of its own, blocking essentially all of serotonin's effects.

At **5-HT7** receptors, aripiprazole was a weak partial agonist with very low intrinsic activity, effectively also functioning as an antagonist of serotonin's actions.

### Functional Effects at Dopamine Receptors: The Functional Selectivity Story

This is where the paper gets most conceptually interesting, and most relevant to the BioChemCore program's focus on GPCR signaling.

At **D2L receptors**, aripiprazole behaved completely differently depending on the cell system:

- In HEK-293 cells stably expressing D2L, aripiprazole was a clear **partial agonist** (EC50 = 0.15 nM; ~75% intrinsic activity), replicating prior reports.
- In D2L-transfected CHO cells, aripiprazole was a less potent partial agonist (EC50 = 18.3 nM; intrinsic activity ~75%).
- In MES-23.5 cells stably expressing D2L, aripiprazole had **no detectable agonist activity** on K+ currents — it was a pure antagonist of both dopamine and quinpirole.
- In GTPγS binding studies in D2L-CHO cells, aripiprazole had no effect on its own and blocked the effects of dopamine and quinpirole like an antagonist.
- In cAMP assays with stable D2L cells, aripiprazole showed no inhibition of forskolin-stimulated cAMP production at any concentration tested.

So the same drug, at the same receptor, is a partial agonist in two cell lines and a pure antagonist in three others. This is the essence of **functional selectivity** (also called "biased agonism" or "agonist trafficking"). The idea is that the same receptor can adopt different conformational states depending on which G proteins and signaling partners are present in a given cell type. A drug like aripiprazole may selectively stabilize one conformation over another, leading to distinct downstream signaling outputs that depend on the cellular context — not just the receptor-drug interaction in isolation.

At **D3 receptors**, aripiprazole was a strong agonist in MES-23.5 cells (activating K+ currents comparably to quinpirole), and these effects were completely blocked by the D2-like antagonist sulpiride.

At **D4 receptors** in the same cell line, aripiprazole behaved as a partial agonist for both K+ current inhibition and cAMP production (EC50 = 16 nM for cAMP, versus 363 nM for dopamine). Thus aripiprazole is more potent than dopamine at D4.

### Key quantitative summary

| Receptor | Functional Role | EC50 / Ki |
|---|---|---|
| 5-HT2B | Inverse agonist (decreases basal PI) | EC50 ~11 nM |
| hD2L | Partial agonist (some systems) / antagonist (others) | Ki ~0.74 nM |
| D3 | Agonist | Ki ~1–3 nM |
| D4 | Partial agonist | EC50 16 nM |
| 5-HT2A | Antagonist in vivo (very low intrinsic activity) | Ki ~8.7 nM |
| 5-HT2C | Partial agonist (high intrinsic activity ~82%) | EC50 ~23–26 nM |
| 5-HT1A | Low-potency partial agonist | EC50 329 nM |
| 5-HT6 | Antagonist | Ki ~570 nM |
| 5-HT7 | Weak partial agonist / antagonist | Ki ~10.3 nM |

## 5. Significance & Implications

This paper was among the first to systematically demonstrate that a clinically used drug produces fundamentally different functional outcomes at the same receptor subtype depending on cellular context. This concept — functional selectivity or biased agonism — has since become one of the most important ideas in GPCR pharmacology and drug development.

For your BioChemCore program, this paper is directly relevant in several ways.

The 5-HT2A receptor is your primary target for MD simulation. This paper provides a critical pharmacological baseline: it establishes that 5-HT2A couples to Gq (measured via PI hydrolysis), that its receptor reserve substantially changes the apparent agonist/antagonist profile of a drug, and that the transmembrane binding pocket is sensitive to the intracellular signaling environment (consistent with what you'd expect to see in MD trajectories — where TM helix movements reflect different conformational states). The paper also quantifies how aripiprazole interacts with 5-HT2A differently than the full agonist serotonin, which connects directly to understanding what structural features of the receptor are important for ligand efficacy.

The functional selectivity findings provide a key conceptual bridge to what MD simulations are now being used to investigate: the structural basis of biased signaling. When aripiprazole is an agonist in one cell but an antagonist in another, the molecular-level explanation involves which receptor conformations are stabilized by drug binding. MD simulations can in principle capture those conformational populations and explain why a particular ligand selectively activates one G protein pathway over another. Papers like Peeters 2025 and Jastrzębski 2025 in your reading list are directly building on this idea.

From a clinical-translational perspective, aripiprazole's complex pharmacology explains several favorable features: minimal EPS (due to partial rather than full D2 blockade), low weight gain (moderate H1 affinity plus 5-HT2C partial agonism), no cardiac valve risk (5-HT2B inverse agonism), and potentially antidepressant-adjacent effects (5-HT1A partial agonism and 5-HT2A antagonism in prefrontal cortex). The drug is now used not just for schizophrenia but for bipolar disorder, treatment-resistant depression, and other conditions — a breadth that the unusual receptor profile described here helps explain.

## 6. Limitations & Open Questions

**The cell-line problem.** The most important caveat in this paper is that all functional assays are conducted in heterologous expression systems — cell lines engineered to overexpress a single receptor. These do not accurately replicate the G protein complement, receptor reserve, accessory protein environment, or membrane lipid composition of actual neurons. The authors themselves acknowledge that the in vitro assay systems "may not accurately reflect the actions of the drug in vivo." The fact that aripiprazole is an antagonist in some D2L systems and a partial agonist in others is real, but translating that to brain circuit-level predictions requires caution.

**Sodium ion sensitivity.** The paper identifies that aripiprazole's affinity at D2 receptors is strongly modulated by sodium, which makes comparison across published studies difficult. This is a structural biology point worth remembering: sodium ions occupy an allosteric site in D2-family GPCRs and stabilize inactive receptor conformations, reducing agonist affinity. Aripiprazole's unusual sensitivity to this effect suggests it binds in a way that is conformationally distinct from classic full agonists — relevant to understanding what you'd want to capture in an MD simulation.

**Receptor reserve ambiguity.** Whether a drug is a partial agonist or a pure antagonist in a native tissue depends heavily on receptor reserve (the proportion of "spare" receptors). The authors make a reasonable argument that in most brain regions, 5-HT2A receptor reserve is low, making aripiprazole effectively an antagonist in vivo — but this is inferred rather than directly demonstrated.

**No structural data.** In 2003, no crystal structure of any Class A GPCR had been solved (the first, rhodopsin, had just been done in 2000 but the 5-HT2A structure was decades away). The mechanistic explanations for functional selectivity were pharmacological and hypothetical. Modern MD simulations and cryo-EM structures have since provided the structural basis for what this paper described behaviorally.

**Temporal resolution.** The paper captures equilibrium binding and steady-state signaling, but not kinetic aspects — how quickly aripiprazole associates and dissociates from receptors, which is now understood to be clinically important ("residence time" — see Ortiz 2026 in your reading list).

## 7. Key Terms & Concepts

**Partial agonist:** A drug that binds to a receptor and activates it, but produces a submaximal response even at full receptor occupancy — it activates the receptor less fully than the endogenous ligand (here, dopamine or serotonin).

**Inverse agonist:** A drug that binds to the same site as an agonist but suppresses the receptor's constitutive (baseline, ligand-independent) activity, pushing signaling below its resting level. Aripiprazole is an inverse agonist at 5-HT2B.

**Functional selectivity (biased agonism):** The phenomenon where a drug produces different signaling outcomes at the same receptor depending on the cellular context (e.g., which G proteins or arrestins are present). The same drug can be an agonist in one cell and an antagonist in another because it preferentially stabilizes different receptor conformational states.

**Receptor reserve (spare receptors):** A condition where a tissue contains more receptors than are needed to produce a maximal response. High receptor reserve means even partial occupancy achieves a full effect; it also means a weak partial agonist can appear more efficacious than in a low-reserve system.

**Ki (inhibition constant):** The equilibrium dissociation constant for a drug competing with a radioligand for a receptor binding site. Lower Ki = higher affinity. Measured by competition radioligand binding assays.

**PI hydrolysis:** Hydrolysis of phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP2) by phospholipase C, triggered by Gq-coupled receptor activation. Produces inositol phosphates (IP1, IP2, IP3) and diacylglycerol (DAG) as second messengers. Used here as a functional readout of 5-HT2A, 5-HT2B, and 5-HT2C signaling.

**GTPγS binding:** A functional assay measuring G protein activation. [35S]-GTPγS is a non-hydrolyzable analog of GTP that gets incorporated into Gα subunits upon receptor activation. Increased GTPγS binding = receptor is being activated and triggering G protein signaling.
