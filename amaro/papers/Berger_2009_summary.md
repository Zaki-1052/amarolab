# Summary: The Expanded Biology of Serotonin

**Citation:** Berger M, Gray JA, Roth BL. *The Expanded Biology of Serotonin.* Annu Rev Med. 2009;60:355–366. doi:10.1146/annurev.med.60.042307.110802

---

## One-Sentence Takeaway

Serotonin is far more than a brain neurotransmitter — it is a whole-body signaling molecule that regulates cardiovascular function, gastrointestinal motility, metabolic homeostasis, and even organ development, largely through a family of 15 receptor subtypes expressed throughout virtually every organ system.

---

## Background & Motivation

Most people associate serotonin with mood and depression, and with good reason — SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) are among the most prescribed psychiatric drugs in the world. But that framing drastically undersells the molecule's reach. Serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine, or 5-HT) was first isolated in 1948 as a vasoconstrictor in serum, and only later recognized as a neurotransmitter. The irony is that less than one in a million CNS neurons actually produce serotonin, and the vast majority of total body serotonin — roughly 95% — is found outside the brain entirely, synthesized and stored in intestinal enterochromaffin cells.

The 15 known serotonin receptors are grouped into seven families based on their signaling mechanisms. Nearly all of them are expressed in peripheral tissues as well as in the CNS. This means that any drug targeting serotonin receptors will, to some degree, affect organ systems beyond the brain. For years this was treated primarily as a problem (side effects to minimize). This paper reframes it: understanding what each receptor subtype does across multiple organ systems is both a pharmacological challenge and an enormous therapeutic opportunity.

The motivation behind this review is therefore twofold. First, it consolidates emerging research showing just how pervasive serotonergic regulation is across the body. Second, it makes a practical argument: drug developers and clinicians who do not account for peripheral serotonin biology will be blindsided by side effects, miss therapeutic opportunities, or both.

---

## Approach & Methods

This is a narrative review paper, not a primary research study. Berger, Gray, and Roth synthesize findings from dozens of experimental sources — receptor knockout mouse models, receptor-subtype-selective drugs, human case studies, and pharmacological intervention trials — to build a system-by-system picture of serotonin's roles in the body.

The authors are well positioned to do this. Bryan Roth's lab at UNC is one of the leading groups in serotonin receptor pharmacology and drug screening, and the paper draws on the lab's own work identifying 5-HT2B receptor agonism as a cause of valvular heart disease (a finding with real clinical impact, discussed below). The review is organized by organ system, with each section asking: what does serotonin do here, what receptors mediate it, what happens when those receptors are dysregulated, and are there therapeutic implications?

## Key Findings

### The brain: pleiotropic control through receptor co-expression

In the CNS, serotonin neurons originate almost entirely in the raphe nuclei of the brainstem and project diffusely throughout the cortex, limbic system, midbrain, and spinal cord. Because virtually every brain region expresses multiple serotonin receptor subtypes, and because individual neurons can express multiple receptors simultaneously, the same serotonin signal can have very different effects depending on where and what receptors are activated. Layer V pyramidal neurons, for example, express both 5-HT1A (inhibitory) and 5-HT2A (excitatory) receptors, which exert opposing effects on firing. This is directly relevant to psychedelic pharmacology: the 5-HT2A receptor is the primary target of classical psychedelics including psilocybin and LSD, and its expression on these cortical pyramidal neurons is thought to drive the dramatic perceptual changes these drugs produce.

The authors note that the lack of a one-to-one correspondence between specific receptors and specific behaviors explains why serotonergic drugs inevitably have effects across multiple behavioral domains, even when highly subtype-selective. The 5-HT2C receptor, for instance, regulates not only anxiety but reward processing, locomotion, appetite, and energy balance.

### Vascular biology: vasoconstriction, platelets, and a novel signaling mechanism

Serotonin plays opposing roles in different vascular beds — it causes vasoconstriction or vasodilation depending on which receptors are expressed in the surrounding smooth muscle. Activation of 5-HT1B receptors on cerebral blood vessels causes vasodilation, which is part of how triptan drugs relieve migraines.

Platelets are major players in peripheral serotonin biology. They lack the enzymes to synthesize serotonin themselves, so they take it up from plasma via the serotonin transporter (SERT) and store it in dense granules. During platelet activation, this stored serotonin is released, promoting aggregation and vasoconstriction. SSRIs, by blocking SERT, deplete platelet serotonin stores — which is why SSRIs can increase bleeding time. This is a clinically meaningful side effect that most patients and some clinicians underappreciate.

One of the most striking findings covered in this section is that serotonin can act entirely independently of membrane receptors through a process called serotonylation: it is covalently attached to small GTPases and other intracellular signaling proteins via tissue transglutaminase. This constitutively activates G protein-dependent signaling pathways. The classical model of serotonin as a ligand binding reversibly to a receptor at the cell surface is therefore incomplete — serotonin can also function as a post-translational modifier of intracellular proteins, a fundamentally different signaling paradigm.

### Cardiac function: 5-HT2B, valvular disease, and the fenfluramine lesson

Serotonin regulates electrical conduction, valvular function, and post-injury remodeling in the heart. The 5-HT4 receptor increases in failing cardiac ventricles and may contribute to pathological remodeling in congestive heart failure. More consequential for drug safety, 5-HT2B receptor activation on valvular interstitial cells is mitogenic — it promotes cell proliferation and increases valve leaflet area. This is the direct mechanism behind the cardiac valvulopathy caused by the diet drug fenfluramine ("fen-phen"), whose active metabolite norfenfluramine is a potent 5-HT2B agonist.

Roth's group screened a library of drugs and found that several ergoline compounds (pergolide, cabergoline — used in Parkinson's disease) and MDMA ("Ecstasy") are also potent 5-HT2B agonists. They predicted valvular disease with chronic use, and subsequent clinical studies confirmed this prediction. This work led to the recommendation that all candidate drugs be screened for 5-HT2B agonism before clinical use. Mice lacking 5-HT2B die of cardiac defects or develop dilated cardiomyopathy, demonstrating that this receptor is essential not just for adult valve function but for cardiac development itself.

### Pulmonary artery hypertension: serotonylation in the lung

In pulmonary artery hypertension (PAH), hypoxia elevates circulating serotonin levels, which increases 5-HT2B signaling on pulmonary artery endothelial cells, driving vascular remodeling and resistance. Mice lacking 5-HT2B do not develop PAH. One patient with a gain-of-function 5-HT2B mutation spontaneously developed PAH, suggesting elevated receptor signaling alone is sufficient to cause the disease. Interestingly, SSRIs can block the development of PAH in animal models — suggesting that intracellular (transporter-dependent) serotonin accumulation, not extracellular receptor binding, may be the causal mechanism. This reinforces the importance of the receptor-independent serotonylation mechanism described above.

Serotonin also modulates the brainstem pre-Botzinger complex via 5-HT4 receptors to regulate respiratory rhythm. Remarkably, serotonergic abnormalities are found in roughly 50% of infants who died from sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS) — these infants have more medullary serotonin neurons but lower SERT and 5-HT1A expression, potentially impairing the CO2 chemosensory response that normally rescues breathing during apnea.

### Endocrinology and metabolism: weight, glucose, and organ regeneration

Hypothalamic 5-HT2C receptors are major regulators of energy balance — they act through melanocortin pathways and drive sympathetic innervation of brown adipose tissue. 5-HT2C agonists have therefore been investigated as treatments for obesity and type 2 diabetes. Serotonin also regulates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, contributing to stress responses.

Peripheral serotonin participates in organ development and regeneration. It is synthesized within the developing mammary gland as part of an autocrine-paracrine loop needed for normal development, and regulates epithelial tight junctions and milk release in adults. In a striking finding, platelet-derived serotonin signals through 5-HT2A and 5-HT2B receptors to promote liver regeneration after hepatectomy or volume loss.

### Gastrointestinal system: the enteric serotonin reservoir

About 95% of total body serotonin lives in the gut, produced by enterochromaffin cells lining the intestinal wall. Serotonin is released the moment food enters the body — even tongue taste-bud stimulation triggers serotonin release onto sensory afferent nerves. In the gut, serotonin drives peristaltic waves, controls intestinal secretion, and regulates pancreatic enzyme release. The 5-HT3 and 5-HT4 receptors are major clinical targets here: 5-HT3 antagonists (like ondansetron) are first-line antiemetics, and 5-HT3/5-HT4 modulators are used in irritable bowel syndrome. The chemotherapy-induced nausea that ondansetron treats is mechanistically driven by excessive GI serotonin activating vagal afferents that signal brainstem vomiting centers.

### Pain, reproduction, and beyond

Serotonin modulates pain at multiple levels simultaneously — sensitizing peripheral nociceptors, modulating incoming spinal signals via descending raphe projections, and shaping the psychological perception of pain in cortical and limbic regions. This multisite involvement explains why serotonergic antidepressants can treat chronic pain conditions.

Serotonin levels rise tenfold in preeclamptic pregnancies, and this elevated serotonin (from increased platelet activation and reduced monoamine oxidase metabolism) may cause the pathological vasoconstriction seen in preeclampsia. The 5-HT2A receptor drives uterine contractions favoring sperm transport, and 5-HT2A-induced collagenase expression promotes uterine involution after delivery. In the genitourinary system, serotonin delays ejaculation and orgasm on net (via 5-HT2C and 5-HT1B), which is why SSRIs are used off-label for premature ejaculation.

## Significance & Implications

This paper matters in two distinct ways, and both are directly relevant to the BioChemCore program.

First, it establishes the foundational biology of serotonin receptors as a family — the diversity of receptor subtypes, their tissue distributions, their opposing signaling effects, and their roles in physiology and disease. If you are working with the 5-HT2A receptor in the context of psychedelic pharmacology and molecular dynamics simulations, this paper gives you the ecological context: you are simulating one member of a 15-receptor family, each with distinct signaling properties, each embedded in specific membrane and cellular environments. The 5-HT2A receptor is expressed prominently on Layer V cortical pyramidal neurons alongside 5-HT1A (which has opposing effects), it drives uterine contractions and liver regeneration, and it may mediate perceptual distortions in psychosis as well as psychedelic states. Its biology is far richer than "it's the psychedelic receptor."

Second, the paper illustrates why receptor selectivity is so hard to achieve and so consequential. The valvular heart disease story is a perfect case study: fenfluramine was a functional drug for weight loss that killed people because nobody checked its metabolite's activity at 5-HT2B. The lesson is that off-target receptor activity — particularly at receptors embedded in different membrane environments with different lipid compositions and interacting proteins — can be catastrophic. For MD simulations, this argues for characterizing the receptor-specific lipid microenvironment, because the 5-HT2A receptor's behavior in a neuronal post-synaptic membrane (rich in cholesterol and specific phospholipids) may differ meaningfully from how it behaves in other tissues.

The concept of serotonylation is also worth flagging for its conceptual novelty: it reveals that a neurotransmitter can function as a post-translational modification enzyme substrate, completely bypassing the classical receptor model. This is a reminder that the receptor-ligand paradigm, while central to pharmacology and MD simulation rationale, does not capture the full spectrum of a signaling molecule's biology.

## Limitations & Open Questions

Being a 2009 review paper, this work predates major structural advances in 5-HT receptor biology. The first crystal structures of the 5-HT2B receptor were published in 2013 and the 5-HT2A receptor only more recently, meaning the structural understanding of how receptor subtype differences translate to differential downstream signaling was still emerging. The authors acknowledge the complexity of serotonergic regulation but do not deeply engage with the mechanistic basis — the review is fundamentally descriptive and organ-system-focused rather than mechanistic.

The paper also does not address functional selectivity (biased agonism) — the finding that different ligands at the same receptor can preferentially activate different downstream signaling pathways (e.g., Gq vs. beta-arrestin at 5-HT2A). This concept, which is now central to psychedelic pharmacology research, was gaining traction around 2009 but is not discussed here. The distinction between G protein signaling and arrestin-mediated signaling at the 5-HT2A receptor is particularly relevant to understanding why some compounds are hallucinogenic and others (like TBG or tabernanthalog) are not.

Finally, the serotonylation mechanism remains incompletely understood even now. The paper describes it as a novel finding and flags it as an open question — that is still largely true. Its physiological importance relative to canonical receptor signaling in most tissues is not established.

## Key Terms & Concepts

**5-HT (5-hydroxytryptamine / serotonin):** A monoamine signaling molecule synthesized from the amino acid tryptophan; it functions as both a neurotransmitter in the CNS and a circulating paracrine/endocrine signal in peripheral organs.

**Serotonin receptor subtypes (5-HT1 through 5-HT7):** A family of 15 distinct G protein-coupled receptors (plus one ligand-gated ion channel, 5-HT3) with different tissue distributions and opposing or complementary signaling effects; the 5-HT2A subtype is the canonical target of classical psychedelics.

**SERT (serotonin transporter):** A transmembrane protein that clears serotonin from synapses or from circulation back into cells; SSRIs work by blocking SERT, and SERT is the primary mechanism by which platelets accumulate serotonin.

**Serotonylation:** A receptor-independent mechanism in which serotonin is covalently attached to small GTPases (e.g., RhoA) by tissue transglutaminase, constitutively activating G protein signaling without requiring membrane receptor binding.

**Enterochromaffin cells:** Specialized epithelial cells lining the GI tract that synthesize and store approximately 95% of total body serotonin; they release it in response to mechanical and chemical stimulation from food.

**5-HT2B receptor:** A serotonin receptor subtype expressed on cardiac valvular interstitial cells and pulmonary artery endothelium whose activation is mitogenic; chronic agonism causes valvular heart disease (the fenfluramine problem) and pulmonary artery hypertension.

**Raphe nuclei:** A cluster of brainstem nuclei that contain virtually all CNS serotonin-producing neurons; they project diffusely throughout the brain and spinal cord, giving serotonin its global modulatory influence on neural circuits.
