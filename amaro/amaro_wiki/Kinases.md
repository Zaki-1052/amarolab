# Kinases

## General Background[edit](</mediawiki/index.php?title=Kinases&action=edit&section=1> "Edit section: General Background")]

The Human Kinome - the classifications refer to the relatedness of the kinase catalytic domains to one another: 

[Kinome Tree](</mediawiki/images/2/27/Kinome_Tree.pdf> "Kinome Tree.pdf")

[Human Kinome Science Paper](</mediawiki/images/1/1f/Science2002_humankinome.pdf> "Science2002 humankinome.pdf") [SI](</mediawiki/images/1/1f/Science2002_humankinome.pdf> "Science2002 humankinome.pdf")

[Original Kinase Classification Paper](</mediawiki/images/1/1a/Fasebj1995_hankshunter_originalkinaseclassification.pdf> "Fasebj1995 hankshunter originalkinaseclassification.pdf")

The paper above has the classifications determined by Tony Hunter (Salk Inst) and the sequence regions that are still referred to today for the kinase catalytic domain. 

[TIBS paper 2011](</mediawiki/images/d/d3/Trendsbiochemsci2011_evolutionofdynamicregulatoryproteins_taylor.pdf> "Trendsbiochemsci2011 evolutionofdynamicregulatoryproteins taylor.pdf")

The paper above has a very nice introduction to the various structural features for the kinase catalytic domain; PKA-C is the example. It first describes the regulatory and catalytic "spines" defined by the Taylor group. This is an important paper to read for the projects. 

[Nature2007](</mediawiki/images/f/f3/Nature2007_patternsofsomaticmutationhumancancergenomes.pdf> "Nature2007 patternsofsomaticmutationhumancancergenomes.pdf")

An interesting article about which kinase mutations are considered cancer drivers or passengers. It's not necessary reading but it was important in shaping the way the pharma industry looked at particular kinases as cancer targets.
