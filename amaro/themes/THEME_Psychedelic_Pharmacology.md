# Master Thematic Summary: Psychedelic Pharmacology & Therapeutics

## 1. Theme Overview

This group of papers traces the molecular pharmacology of classical psychedelics from their receptor-level interactions all the way through to clinical outcomes in patients with treatment-resistant depression, anxiety, and schizophrenia. The 5-HT2A serotonin receptor — the very protein Zara is simulating in BioChemCore — is the central protagonist across all ten papers. Together, these papers establish what the receptor does, why it matters therapeutically, and how understanding its precise conformational dynamics could enable the next generation of safer, more effective drugs.

## 2. The Narrative Arc

### The Foundational Problem: Dirty Drugs Work Best (Roth 2004)

The story begins not with psychedelics but with a counterintuitive observation about psychiatric drugs in general. Roth et al. 2004 made the case that schizophrenia and depression, as polygenic multi-circuit diseases, consistently respond better to pharmacologically complex "dirty" drugs — clozapine, tricyclics, ECT — than to selective single-target compounds. The failure of dozens of "magic bullet" drugs in clinical trials supported the idea that deliberately engaging multiple molecular targets ("magic shotguns") is a more realistic strategy. This 2004 perspective established the conceptual foundation for everything that followed: if a disease is distributed across many signaling nodes, you need a drug that reaches multiple nodes simultaneously. It also introduced 5-HT2A as a central hub in both antipsychotic and hallucinogenic pharmacology, and formalized the concept of functional selectivity — the idea that different ligands at the same receptor can activate different downstream pathways.

### The Clinical Evidence Arrives (Griffiths 2016, Carhart-Harris 2018)

A decade later, the clinical promise crystallized. Griffiths et al. 2016 published the largest and most rigorous trial to that date: 51 cancer patients with depression and anxiety received a high-dose or low-dose psilocybin in a double-blind crossover design. The effects were startling — 78% showed clinically significant antidepressant response at 6-month follow-up, and intensity of the acute mystical experience was a strong statistical mediator of outcome. This was the paper that helped psilocybin earn FDA Breakthrough Therapy designation. Carhart-Harris et al. 2018 extended the story to treatment-resistant depression: 20 patients who had failed a median of 4.6 medications showed Cohen's d effect sizes of 2.3 at 5 weeks — more than four times the effect sizes typical of conventional antidepressants. Both papers converge on the same mechanistic implication: 5-HT2A agonism in cortical circuitry produces sustained therapeutic restructuring, not merely a transient symptomatic lift.

Critically, both papers also surfaced what would become the central unresolved tension: the acute mystical or psychological experience correlates with long-term benefit. Does the trip itself matter, or is it just the pharmacological downstream cascade? This question is never fully resolved in the literature, but it sets the agenda for everything that follows.

### Decoding the Receptor: Polypharmacology and Signaling (McClure-Begley 2022, Jain 2025)

With clinical proof-of-concept in hand, the field's attention turned to mechanism. McClure-Begley and Roth 2022 offered a comprehensive review of what was known structurally and pharmacologically, including the key X-ray finding that LSD's diethylamide moiety forms a "lid" over the 5-HT2AR binding pocket via Leu229 in extracellular loop 2 — explaining LSD's notoriously slow dissociation kinetics. They also catalogued the alarming breadth of 5-HT2B activation across psychedelics, flagging cardiac valvulopathy risk for chronic use. A human/rodent species difference at position 242 (Ser in humans, Ala in rodents) was identified as a reason rodent assays may systematically underestimate human potency.

Jain et al. 2025 resolved the polypharmacology question more definitively than any prior work. Screening 41 classical psychedelics against 318 GPCRs in the PRESTO-Tango assay, they showed that lysergamides like LSD hit an average of 21 receptor targets, tryptamines 14, and phenethylamines 12. Two major findings stood out. First, psychedelics do not activate TrkB kinase directly — a claim that had appeared in prior literature and that Jain et al. refuted with a 450-kinase screen of LSD and live-cell reporter assays for psilocybin. Second, in vivo hallucinogenic potency correlated most tightly with Gaq/Ga11/Ga15 signaling pathways at 5-HT2AR, specifically reaching r = 0.97 for the head-twitch response data. Crucially, all tested psychedelics activated 5-HT2BR — a cardiac concern that Jain 2025 establishes as universal to the class, not compound-specific.

### The Phenomenology Comparison (Ley 2023)

Ley et al. 2023 stepped back from mechanism to answer a practical clinical question: are mescaline, LSD, and psilocybin actually different subjective drugs? In a rigorous double-blind crossover study with 32 healthy adults, the answer was largely no — at psychoactive-equivalent doses (500 mg mescaline / 100 µg LSD / 20 mg psilocybin), all three produced indistinguishable subjective experiences on validated psychometric instruments. The one meaningful difference was duration: mescaline lasted about 11 hours, LSD 8 hours, psilocybin 5 hours — differences explained by pharmacokinetics rather than pharmacodynamics. This paper also produced an unexpected finding: mescaline and LSD elevated plasma oxytocin significantly while psilocybin did not, raising questions about whether different compounds, despite equivalent subjective effects, might work better for different therapeutic contexts.

### The Mechanistic Framework (Siegel 2026)

Siegel et al. 2026 offered the most comprehensive mechanistic synthesis to date, organizing the evidence around two temporal phases. During the acute experience, 5-HT2A activation desynchronizes cortical oscillations — neurons fire less in phase with local field potentials, EEG entropy rises, and the default mode network (the brain's self-model) fragments. This creates a window of genuine neural flexibility rather than just noise. After the drug clears, a subacute neuroplasticity window emerges: dendritic spine growth, upregulation of plasticity genes, BDNF/TrkB pathway activation, and synaptogenesis in the prefrontal cortex — a process that can persist for weeks and that may be the actual site of therapeutic consolidation. The implication is that the "trip" creates the conditions for lasting change, and the subsequent therapy/integration session is where those conditions are actually used.

### Engineering the Next Generation: Psychoplastogens (Cameron 2021, Aarrestad 2025, Tuck 2025)

The most recent arc in the narrative is drug design. If the full agonist psychedelic experience is therapeutically powerful but clinically problematic, can you strip away the hallucinogenic component while keeping the neuroplasticity? Cameron et al. 2021 answered yes, via function-oriented synthesis. Starting from ibogaine and systematically dismantling it to find the active pharmacophore, they designed TBG (tabernanthalog) — a compound with a 100-fold reduced hERG toxicity versus ibogaine, no head-twitch response, and potent neuroplasticity promotion that was ketanserin-blockable (confirming 5-HT2A dependence). TBG also reduced binge drinking and heroin-seeking in rodents.

Aarrestad et al. 2025 refined the mechanistic picture of TBG dramatically. Using 5-HT2A knockout mice, fiber photometry for real-time glutamate measurement, single-nucleus RNA sequencing, and PET receptor occupancy imaging in pigs, they showed that TBG requires 5-HT2A but — unlike classic psychedelics — does not trigger a glutamate burst in the PFC and does not induce immediate early gene (IEG) expression. Classic psychedelics like 5-MeO-DMT strongly activate c-Fos and NPAS4; TBG produces no IEG signal at all, and neither do two other nonhallucinogenic psychoplastogens. Yet TBG still grows dendritic spines and produces antidepressant behavior. The conclusion was striking: IEG activation and the glutamate burst are not required for structural plasticity. They may be more closely linked to hallucinogenesis than to therapeutic neuroplasticity.

Tuck et al. 2025 took this precision to its logical extreme: starting from LSD itself, they moved two atoms to eliminate a hydrogen bond between the indole N-H and Ser242/Gly238 in the binding pocket, creating JRT. This single geometric change shifted the receptor into a non-hallucinogenic conformation (confirmed with the psychLight biosensor and the S242A mutant version), accelerated drug dissociation 10-fold, and eliminated the head-twitch response entirely in mice. Yet JRT still produced a 46% increase in dendritic spine density in the mPFC after a single dose, rescued stress-induced cortical atrophy, improved reversal learning, and produced antidepressant and anti-anhedonic effects in validated models. Strikingly, LSD's gene expression signature in mouse PFC overlapped significantly with schizophrenia risk genes — but JRT's did not. Two atoms. One angstrom of spatial shift. Completely different transcriptional and behavioral profile.

## 3. Key Concepts and Converging Evidence

**5-HT2AR as the master switch.** Every paper in this group agrees: the 5-HT2A receptor is the essential molecular target for classical psychedelics and psychoplastogens. Ketanserin blockade abolishes the therapeutic and hallucinogenic effects of all tested compounds. 5-HT2A knockout mice show no response to TBG (Aarrestad 2025). This convergence across pharmacological, genetic, and clinical approaches is about as strong as mechanistic evidence gets in neuroscience.

**The neuroplasticity cascade.** Multiple papers independently converge on a shared downstream pathway: 5-HT2AR agonism drives Gq signaling, which drives IP3/DAG production, which activates downstream kinase cascades (including mTOR and eventually TrkB), which drives dendritic spine growth and BDNF release. This is confirmed in vitro (Cameron 2021, Tuck 2025), in vivo in rodents (Aarrestad 2025, Tuck 2025), and inferred from clinical neuroplasticity biomarkers (Siegel 2026). The mTOR and TrkB requirements are consistent across multiple psychoplastogens.

**Polypharmacology is a feature, not a bug.** Roth 2004 established the theoretical case; Jain 2025 provided the empirical map. The clinical durability of psychedelics may arise partly from their ability to simultaneously engage multiple receptor systems. Tuck 2025 echoes this point: JRT's activity across 5-HT2A, 5-HT2C, 5-HT1A, and 5-HT7 may be why it outperforms LSD in some neuroplasticity measures despite being a partial rather than full agonist.

**5-HT2BR cardiac risk is universal.** McClure-Begley 2022 flagged it; Jain 2025 confirmed it across 41 compounds without exception. Every classical psychedelic tested activates 5-HT2BR. Tuck 2025 explicitly lists it as a safety liability for JRT. This is not a niche concern — it is a field-wide challenge that any psychedelic-inspired drug must address, particularly for repeat-dose protocols like microdosing.

**The acute experience correlates with, but may not cause, therapeutic outcome.** Griffiths 2016 found mystical experience intensity mediates outcomes statistically. Carhart-Harris 2018 found "insightfulness" predicts remission. Yet Aarrestad 2025 shows TBG produces antidepressant effects without any hallucinogenic experience at all. These findings are not contradictory but suggest the relationship is complex: the mystical experience may be one path to therapeutic benefit, not the only one.

## 4. Active Debates and Unresolved Questions

**Is the hallucinogenic experience necessary for therapeutic benefit?**

This is the most consequential unresolved question in the field. Griffiths 2016 and Carhart-Harris 2018 found that the quality of the acute experience predicts outcomes, which suggests it matters. Siegel 2026 acknowledges the subjective experience may activate a window of neuroplasticity but also notes that NHPAs (non-hallucinogenic psychedelic analogs) produce comparable plasticity without a trip. Aarrestad 2025 and Cameron 2021 show TBG works in rodents without any hallucinogenic signature. However, all NHPA therapeutic evidence remains preclinical. No human trial has established that a non-hallucinogenic psychoplastogen produces comparable antidepressant outcomes to psilocybin in treatment-resistant patients.

The honest current answer: in rodents, no. In humans, we do not know yet.

**Does psychedelic neuroplasticity require the glutamate burst and IEG activation?**

Aarrestad 2025 argues definitively that it does not — TBG grows spines without glutamate bursts or IEG induction. But this finding is limited to a 1-hour IEG measurement window, and Aarrestad et al. acknowledge that delayed IEG expression (4-6 hours) was not assessed. Whether spine stabilization over days requires some later transcriptional program (IEGs are classically involved in synaptic tagging) remains genuinely open. Multiple papers (McClure-Begley 2022, Siegel 2026) describe IEG induction as part of the plasticity cascade for classic psychedelics, but Aarrestad 2025 challenges whether this is a required step or merely an epiphenomenon of full agonism.

**Does TrkB mediate the antidepressant effects of psychedelics?**

Jain 2025 presents the strongest evidence against TrkB as a direct psychedelic target: LSD showed no activity in a 450-kinase screen, and psilocybin/LSD showed no TrkB agonism or allosteric modulation in live-cell assays. But Siegel 2026 and Aarrestad 2025 describe TrkB as activated *downstream* of 5-HT2AR signaling — not directly by the drug. This distinction matters: TrkB may be required for neuroplasticity without being a direct drug target. Aarrestad 2025 confirms TrkB is needed in the downstream cascade for TBG-induced spinogenesis, consistent with Jain 2025's finding that TrkB is not directly bound by the drug. These findings actually complement rather than contradict each other.

**Is Gq signaling sufficient for plasticity, or is beta-arrestin required?**

Multiple papers (McClure-Begley 2022, Jain 2025, Tuck 2025, Siegel 2026) discuss biased agonism as the key design principle for separating hallucinogenic from neuroplastic effects. The working hypothesis is that Gq bias mediates hallucinogenesis while TrkB/beta-arrestin pathways mediate plasticity. But Aarrestad 2025 complicates this: TBG is described as a balanced partial agonist at both Gq and beta-arrestin in BRET assays, yet it lacks hallucinogenic effects and retains plasticity. The precise pathway requirements remain to be defined, and the currently proposed models are not fully consistent across papers.

## 5. Direct BioChemCore Relevance

Your MD simulation of the 5-HT2A receptor is directly informed by virtually every paper in this group. Here is what to extract for your pipeline.

**Which PDB structure to use.** McClure-Begley 2022 discusses the 5-HT2AR crystal structure bound to LSD (from Wacker et al. 2017, Nature Structural & Molecular Biology) and the full signaling complex structure from the Roth lab (2020). The cryo-EM structure of LSD-D2R complex at 2.3 Å is described in Jain 2025. For 5-HT2AR specifically, the experimentally resolved structures with ligands bound are your starting points. Check the PDB for entries from the Roth lab (RCSB entries for HTR2A with LSD, 25CN-NBOMe).

**Key residues to monitor.** Multiple papers converge on the same binding pocket residues:
- **D155 (3.32):** the conserved ionic anchor for amine ligands across most aminergic GPCRs
- **S242 (5.46) and G238 (5.42):** the serines that LSD hydrogen-bonds to; Tuck 2025 shows that eliminating this bond by 1 Angstrom converts LSD to a non-hallucinogen
- **L229 / W151 (3.28):** mediates the "lid" effect that slows LSD dissociation (McClure-Begley 2022)
- **W336 (6.48) and F340 (6.52):** toggle switch residues in the hydrophobic core
- **Y370 (7.43):** conserved aromatic contact in the orthosteric pocket
- **EL2 (extracellular loop 2):** the dynamic loop that closes over the binding pocket and contributes to ligand trapping

**Membrane composition.** The 5-HT2AR is expressed on layer V cortical pyramidal neurons in vivo. A brain-relevant membrane would include phosphatidylcholine (PC) and phosphatidylethanolamine (PE) as major lipid headgroups, with cholesterol at physiological ratios (~30 mol%). Sphingomyelin is relevant for raft-associated signaling contexts. For a first-pass simulation, a POPC/POPE/cholesterol bilayer is appropriate.

**Analysis metrics that matter for this biology.** Based on the pharmacological themes across these papers:
- **RMSD/RMSF of TM helices:** TM5/TM6 outward displacement is the canonical active-state signature for Gq-coupled GPCRs. Track TM6 distance from TM3 to monitor activation state.
- **Ligand contact frequency:** Which residues maintain hydrogen bonds or hydrophobic contacts with the ligand over the trajectory? The S242 H-bond is the key difference between LSD and JRT (Tuck 2025).
- **EL2 dynamics:** Ley 2023 and McClure-Begley 2022 both implicate EL2 in ligand residence time. RMSF of EL2 residues will reveal whether different ligands stabilize or destabilize this cap.
- **ICL3 (intracellular loop 3) conformation:** This is the primary Gq coupling interface. Its conformation reflects whether the receptor is in a G-protein-coupling-competent state.
- **Gq coupling interface contacts:** If simulating the receptor with a Gq heterotrimer (possible with the full signaling complex structure), the alpha5 helix of Galphaq's interaction with the intracellular cavity is the key coupling interface.

**Connecting the simulation to biology.** When you observe TM6 movement in your trajectory, that corresponds to the G-protein coupling event that drives the Gq/IP3/DAG/PKC cascade described in McClure-Begley 2022 and Siegel 2026 — the cascade that ultimately drives dendritic spine growth. When you track ligand contacts at S242, you are directly observing the molecular mechanism that distinguishes hallucinogenic LSD from non-hallucinogenic JRT (Tuck 2025). The simulations are not abstract — they map onto clinical effects described in Griffiths 2016 and Carhart-Harris 2018.

## 6. Recommended Reading Order

Read these summaries in the following order. Each one builds on the one before it.

**1. Roth 2004** — Start here to understand why multi-target pharmacology is a feature rather than a problem in CNS drug design, and to see 5-HT2A introduced as a central node in antipsychotic and hallucinogen pharmacology.

**2. McClure-Begley 2022** — The most comprehensive review of 5-HT2A receptor pharmacology and structure in the group. Read this early to build the structural vocabulary (EL2, S242, Gq coupling, biased agonism) you will need for every subsequent paper.

**3. Griffiths 2016** — The landmark RCT establishing clinical proof-of-concept. Read this to ground yourself in what the receptor pharmacology actually does to patients, and to understand the mystical-experience mediation question.

**4. Carhart-Harris 2018** — The treatment-resistant depression trial that extends Griffiths 2016 to a harder-to-treat population. Introduces the "insightfulness predicts remission" finding and grounds the set-and-setting framework.

**5. Ley 2023** — Read here to understand the cross-compound equivalence of subjective effects, which confirms 5-HT2A agonism as the dominant driver, and to encounter the pharmacokinetic distinctions between compound classes.

**6. Jain 2025** — The most pharmacologically comprehensive paper in the group. Resolves the TrkB controversy and establishes the full polypharmacology map. Essential reading before the drug design papers, because it establishes what all psychedelics share (5-HT2BR activation, Gaq bias) and what varies.

**7. Siegel 2026** — The integrative mechanistic review that builds the two-framework model (desynchronization + neuroplasticity). Best read after the clinical papers and the polypharmacology data, since it synthesizes both.

**8. Cameron 2021** — Introduces the psychoplastogen concept and TBG through function-oriented synthesis. The most methodologically accessible drug design paper in the group.

**9. Aarrestad 2025** — The deepest mechanistic investigation of how TBG works, challenging the glutamate burst and IEG hypotheses. Best appreciated after Cameron 2021 introduces TBG.

**10. Tuck 2025** — The most structurally precise paper in the group. Read last because it requires understanding of receptor binding pocket geometry, biased agonism, and the therapeutic rationale for non-hallucinogenic compounds — all of which are established by the nine papers before it.

## 7. Key Terms Quick Reference

**5-HT2A receptor (5-HT2AR):** A Gq-coupled GPCR for serotonin expressed densely on layer V cortical pyramidal neurons. The primary molecular target of classical psychedelics and all compounds discussed in this group. Its activation drives both hallucinogenic effects and neuroplasticity promotion, distinguished by which downstream pathways are recruited.

**Biased agonism (functional selectivity):** The property of a ligand to preferentially stabilize one receptor conformation and thereby activate one downstream signaling pathway (e.g., Gq) over another (e.g., beta-arrestin). The central design principle for non-hallucinogenic psychedelics — different conformations produce different biology from the same receptor.

**Psychoplastogen:** A compound that promotes rapid, lasting structural neuroplasticity — specifically dendritic spine growth and dendritic arborization in the prefrontal cortex — after a single dose. Classic psychedelics are psychoplastogens; TBG and JRT are nonhallucinogenic psychoplastogens.

**Dendritic spine:** A small actin-rich protrusion on a neuronal dendrite that forms the postsynaptic site of most excitatory synapses. Spine density is reduced in depression and schizophrenia and correlates inversely with disease severity. Regrowth of spines is one measurable correlate of antidepressant action.

**Gq signaling / IP3/DAG cascade:** The canonical downstream pathway activated when 5-HT2AR is engaged by agonists. Gq activates phospholipase C, which cleaves PIP2 into IP3 (mobilizing intracellular calcium) and DAG (activating PKC). Downstream events include mTOR activation, TrkB transactivation, and ultimately cytoskeletal remodeling and spine growth.

**Polypharmacology:** The property of a drug binding and activating multiple molecularly distinct targets simultaneously. Established as a clinically advantageous property for CNS drugs in Roth 2004; mapped exhaustively for psychedelics in Jain 2025 (mean of 21 targets for lysergamides).

**Immediate early genes (IEGs):** Activity-dependent genes (c-Fos, NPAS4, Arc, Nr4a1) rapidly transcribed without new protein synthesis when neurons fire intensely. Long assumed to be required for structural plasticity; Aarrestad 2025 challenges this assumption, showing TBG produces plasticity without proximate IEG activation.

**Default mode network (DMN):** A cortical network active during self-referential thought, rich in 5-HT2A receptors. Its disruption by psychedelics correlates with ego dissolution and altered self-perception; its suppression is associated with depression. Described in Siegel 2026 as the primary site of acute psychedelic desynchronization.

**5-HT2B cardiac valvulopathy:** The well-established mechanism by which chronic 5-HT2BR agonism causes structural damage to heart valve leaflets, leading to regurgitation. First established with fenfluramine and ergot derivatives; Jain 2025 confirms all classical psychedelics tested activate 5-HT2BR, making this a field-wide safety concern for chronic dosing regimens.

**Mystical experience (MEQ30):** A specific category of profoundly altered subjective state during a psychedelic session — characterized by feelings of unity, transcendence of time/space, and noetic quality — measured by the 30-item Mystical Experience Questionnaire. Its intensity predicted therapeutic outcomes in Griffiths 2016 and Carhart-Harris 2018.

**TrkB:** The high-affinity receptor for BDNF. Activated downstream of 5-HT2AR signaling (not directly by psychedelics — Jain 2025). Required for psychoplastogen-induced spine growth (Aarrestad 2025). Part of the neuroplasticity cascade described across Siegel 2026, Aarrestad 2025, and Cameron 2021.

**S242 (position 5.46) in 5-HT2AR:** A serine in the orthosteric binding pocket that forms a hydrogen bond with LSD's indole N-H. A single-residue position whose loss of contact (by shifting two atoms in JRT) converts a hallucinogen to a non-hallucinogen (Tuck 2025). Present in humans but replaced by alanine in rodents, creating cross-species potency differences (McClure-Begley 2022).

**Head-twitch response (HTR):** A rapid, involuntary rotational head movement in rodents induced by 5-HT2A agonists. The primary preclinical proxy for hallucinogenic potential. Absent for TBG (Cameron 2021, Aarrestad 2025) and JRT (Tuck 2025); robust for classic psychedelics and blocked by their respective non-hallucinogenic analogs.

**mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin):** A serine/threonine kinase downstream of 5-HT2AR/TrkB signaling that drives protein synthesis for new synaptic structures. Required for psychoplastogen-induced spine growth; rapamycin (mTOR inhibitor) blocks dendritic spine formation by both classic psychedelics and TBG.
